# Clarity Compass — The Foundation of Scalable Execution

## What Is the Clarity Compass and Why It Comes First

The Clarity Compass is the strategic foundation of the Business Operating System (BOS). It answers the most fundamental question a scaling business must resolve: *Where are we going, and why?* Without this foundation, every other BOS component — OKRs, KPIs, Playbooks, Scorecards — sits on unstable ground. Teams optimize locally. Leaders make inconsistent decisions. Good people leave because they don't know what they're building.

Ryan Deiss's *Get Scalable* framework positions the Clarity Compass as the prerequisite to all execution work. The four components are:

1. **3-Year Target** — Where is the company going?
2. **Company Purpose** — Why does this company exist?
3. **Core Values** — How do we behave to get there?
4. **Strategic Anchors** — What do we focus on, consistently, to win?

This order matters. The 3-Year Target anchors everything in a concrete destination. Purpose provides the emotional fuel. Core Values define the behavioral boundaries. Strategic Anchors determine where the company will concentrate its resources and energy over the next three to five years.

---

## Component 1: The 3-Year Target

The 3-Year Target is a specific, aspirational, time-bound description of where the company will be in exactly three years. It is not a mission statement or a vision board. It is a concrete declaration of future state — specific enough that, three years from now, you will know with certainty whether you achieved it.

Three years is the deliberate timeframe. One year is too short to make significant structural changes. Five years is too distant for operational accountability. Three years sits in the sweet spot: far enough to require transformation, close enough to feel real.

### What Makes a Strong 3-Year Target

A strong 3-Year Target is:
- **Specific**: Includes numbers, market positions, and named outcomes — not adjectives
- **Aspirational**: Requires meaningful change from the current state (not linear extrapolation)
- **Singular**: One clear target, not a list of five ambitions
- **Discussable**: Leaders can explain it to any employee in 30 seconds

| Weak 3-Year Target | Strong 3-Year Target |
|---|---|
| "Be the best in our category" | "$50M ARR, serving 500 enterprise clients in EMEA and North America" |
| "Grow significantly" | "Top 3 market share in mid-market B2B SaaS legal tech, with 40%+ gross margin" |
| "Build a great team" | "200 employees, 90% retention rate, recognized as top employer in our sector" |

### How to Build the 3-Year Target

Start with the question: "What would have to be true about this business in three years for us to consider it a major success?"

Probe four dimensions:
1. **Financial**: Revenue, gross margin, EBITDA, or ARR
2. **Market**: Customer count, market share, geographic reach, segment penetration
3. **Capability**: What must the team be able to do that it cannot do today?
4. **Operational**: How will the company operate? What processes, systems, or quality standards define us?

Draft a single paragraph that combines all four. Test it: can someone who doesn't work here read this and understand exactly what winning looks like?

### The 3-Year Target as a Decision Filter

The power of the 3-Year Target extends beyond planning. It becomes a real-time filter for resource allocation decisions. When a new opportunity arises, the first question is: "Does this move us toward the 3-Year Target or away from it?" This makes what are otherwise political or emotional decisions into strategic ones.

## Component 2: Company Purpose

Company Purpose answers the question: *Why does this business exist beyond making money?* A strong Purpose is not aspirational marketing copy — it is the honest answer to what would be lost from the world if this company disappeared tomorrow.

Purpose is not:
- A customer promise ("We deliver exceptional service")
- A product description ("We build software for X")
- A financial target ("We create shareholder value")

Purpose is the *why* beneath the business. It is the thing that makes the right employees stay and the wrong ones self-select out.

### Why Purpose Drives Execution

Purpose functions as a decision accelerant. When teams encounter ambiguous situations — a customer asks for something out of scope, an employee is asked to cut a corner, a business opportunity conflicts with the company's positioning — Purpose provides the tie-breaking logic. Companies with a genuine Purpose resolve these moments faster and more consistently than those without one.

Ryan Deiss identifies three tests for a genuine Purpose:

1. **The Specificity Test**: Could this Purpose be copy-pasted to a competitor? If yes, it's too generic. "We empower people to achieve their goals" could belong to any company on earth.
2. **The Honesty Test**: Do the founders and leaders actually believe this? Purpose that isn't lived is spotted immediately by employees and customers.
3. **The Decision Test**: Can you name three real decisions this year that would have been different if you didn't have this Purpose? If not, the Purpose isn't shaping behavior.

### Purpose vs. Mission vs. Vision

These terms are often used interchangeably, but in the BOS framework they are distinct:
- **Purpose**: Why we exist (permanent, deep)
- **Mission**: What we do for whom (stable, operational)
- **Vision**: Where we are going (aspirational, evolves)
- **3-Year Target**: Specific near-term destination (updates every 3 years)

The Clarity Compass uses Purpose as the stable foundation and the 3-Year Target as the current destination. Mission and Vision can be separate artifacts if needed, but Purpose and Target are the operative components.

### Drafting Company Purpose

Use this prompt with leadership: *"Complete this sentence honestly: The world is better when _____ because _____."*

Then stress-test it:
- Could your direct competitors say this exact thing? If yes, sharpen it.
- Would your founding team have started this business without this belief? If no, it's not real purpose.
- Does this Purpose shape any actual decision you made last quarter? If no, it's decorative.

## Component 3: Core Values

Core Values are the 3–5 behavioral standards that define how the company operates — specifically, how people make decisions, treat each other, and engage with customers when no policy covers the situation.

Core Values are not aspirations for what the company wishes it were like. They are descriptions of what the company already IS at its best, made explicit so they can be hired for, promoted for, and enforced.

### The Problem with Bad Core Values

Most companies have Core Values. Almost none use them. The values on the wall ("Integrity, Innovation, Excellence") are indistinguishable across 90% of organizations. They are not values — they are brand adjectives.

Bad Core Values:
- Cannot be used to make a specific decision
- Are not grounds for firing or promoting someone
- Cannot distinguish someone who embodies them from someone who doesn't
- Are generic enough to belong to any company

### What Good Core Values Look Like

Good Core Values are behavioral, specific, and occasionally uncomfortable. They should describe behavior that, when violated, would constitute a real breach of who you are as a company.

| Generic (Bad) | Behavioral (Good) |
|---|---|
| "Excellence" | "We ship before we're ready and fix fast — shipping is the action that creates learning" |
| "Integrity" | "We say what we believe to be true, even when it's unwelcome, and we never pretend to agree" |
| "Customer First" | "We don't close a deal we don't believe we can deliver — we turn down revenue to protect trust" |
| "Innovation" | "We question how we've always done it every 90 days — no process is sacred" |

### How Many Core Values

Three to five. Fewer than three and there's not enough definition. More than five and they become unmemorable and unmanageable. The five-and-under constraint forces the leadership team to agree on what actually matters most — the values you'd refuse to compromise even at cost.

### Using Core Values in Operations

Core Values only generate value when they are operationalized:
- **Hiring**: Interview questions designed to reveal whether a candidate embodies each value
- **Onboarding**: New hires learn the values through stories, not slide decks
- **Performance Reviews**: Behaviors assessed against values, not just against outputs
- **Promotion Criteria**: Values embodiment is a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have
- **Firing Criteria**: Sustained violation of Core Values is grounds for exit, regardless of performance metrics

## Component 4: Strategic Anchors

Strategic Anchors are 3–5 areas of sustained competitive focus where the company will build deep advantage over the next 3–5 years. They answer the question: *Where will we concentrate our resources to build an unfair competitive position?*

Strategic Anchors differ from strategic goals. Goals are things you achieve. Anchors are things you commit to building in a sustained way. You don't "complete" a Strategic Anchor — you build capability in it continuously.

### How Strategic Anchors Work

A company's resource allocation always reflects some set of strategic bets — explicit or not. Strategic Anchors make those bets explicit, so that budget decisions, hiring decisions, and product investment decisions can be evaluated against them.

Ryan Deiss identifies three tests for a strong Strategic Anchor:
1. **Differentiation**: Does this represent a meaningful point of difference from competitors? Or is it a baseline expectation in the category?
2. **Sustainability**: Can we continue to invest here over 3–5 years, even when it's inconvenient? Or is this only appealing when the market is favorable?
3. **Leverage**: Does competitive advantage in this area create advantages across the whole business, not just one function?

### Examples of Strategic Anchors

**For a professional services firm:**
- Thought leadership in a specific vertical (e.g., financial services regulation)
- Client retention and expansion (Net Revenue Retention as a strategic bet)
- Talent density in a specific expertise (e.g., M&A advisory)

**For a B2B SaaS company:**
- Data network effects (platform value increases with customer data volume)
- Ecosystem integrations (deep embedding in customer workflows)
- Vertical specialization (e.g., winning in healthcare or legal, not horizontally)

**For a manufacturing business:**
- Supply chain resilience and vertical integration
- Custom engineering capability for complex specifications
- Operational excellence and unit cost leadership

### Strategic Anchors vs. Competitive Advantages

Strategic Anchors are what you're *building*. Competitive Advantages are what you *have*. A company in year two of its anchors may have no visible advantage yet — but that is the point. Anchors represent conviction bets about where the market is going and where the company needs to be when it arrives.

## Building the Clarity Compass: Process and Sequencing

### Who Does This Work

The Clarity Compass is built by the leadership team — the CEO plus the direct reports who run the key functions. It is not outsourced to a consultant, not delegated to HR, and not developed through company-wide surveys. It is a leadership decision that gets communicated broadly, not a democratic process.

### The Clarity Compass Workshop

A focused 2-day offsite is the standard format:

**Day 1:**
- Review current state: Where are we? What changed since last year?
- 3-Year Target: Where are we going? (3 hours)
- Company Purpose: Why do we exist? (2 hours)

**Day 2:**
- Core Values: How do we behave? (2 hours)
- Strategic Anchors: Where do we concentrate? (3 hours)
- Integration check: Do the four components reinforce each other?
- Communication plan: How do we share this with the company?

### Integration Check Questions

Before locking the Clarity Compass, apply these questions:
- Does the Purpose inspire the pursuit of the 3-Year Target — or are they unrelated?
- Do the Core Values describe the behavior needed to execute the Strategic Anchors?
- Are the Strategic Anchors the right bets to reach the 3-Year Target?
- If you hired someone for the 3-Year Target but against your Core Values, would it be a net positive or negative? (The answer must be: negative.)

### Updating the Clarity Compass

The Clarity Compass is not annual. The 3-Year Target updates every three years (or when a material external event changes the landscape). Core Values and Purpose are reviewed, not rewritten — they should be stable. Strategic Anchors are reviewed annually: are we still making these bets? Have we won in any of these areas sufficiently to retire an Anchor and add a new one?

## Clarity Compass Quality Checklist

Before publishing:
- [ ] 3-Year Target is specific enough that you'll know in 3 years if you achieved it
- [ ] 3-Year Target spans at least two dimensions (financial + market or operational)
- [ ] Purpose passes the Specificity Test (could not belong to a competitor)
- [ ] Purpose passes the Decision Test (has changed at least one real decision)
- [ ] 3–5 Core Values (no more, no fewer)
- [ ] Each Core Value is behavioral (describes an action, not an adjective)
- [ ] Each Core Value is hiring/firing criteria, not aspirational decoration
- [ ] 3–5 Strategic Anchors (sustainable, differentiated, leverage-generating)
- [ ] Leadership team can recite the 3-Year Target from memory
- [ ] A communication plan exists to share this with all employees
