# High-Output Team Canvas — Building Teams That Scale

## Why Most Teams Don't Scale

Teams that work brilliantly at 5 people often fall apart at 15. Teams that function well at 15 become chaotic at 50. The reason is rarely a talent problem — it's a structure problem. As teams grow, the informal coordination mechanisms that worked at small scale (everyone knows what everyone else is doing, the founder is in every meeting, decisions happen over lunch) stop working.

Ryan Deiss's *Get Scalable* introduces the **High-Output Team Canvas** as the structured approach to building teams that scale. The underlying premise: **high output from a team is a design problem, not a selection problem**. Hiring great people into a poorly designed team produces mediocre results. Placing average people into a well-designed team produces above-average results.

The canvas is built on three interconnected dimensions: **Capabilities**, **Accountability**, and **Behaviors** — the CAB framework.

## The CAB Framework

### C — Capabilities: What the Team Can Do

Capabilities are the skills, knowledge, tools, processes, and resources available to the team to deliver its outputs. Capability analysis answers the question: *Does this team have everything it needs to do its job at the required level of quality?*

Capabilities exist at two levels:
- **Individual Capabilities**: Skills and knowledge that specific team members hold
- **Organizational Capabilities**: Systems, tools, and processes that the team as a whole can leverage, regardless of which individual is doing the work

The distinction is critical for scaling. Individual capabilities create key-person risk. Organizational capabilities (documented in the Playbook Library, embedded in tools, enforced in processes) scale without adding headcount.

**Capability Categories:**

**Technical Skills**: Domain-specific expertise required to perform the work (e.g., for a sales team: discovery questioning, proposal writing, contract negotiation; for an engineering team: specific languages, architectures, testing methodologies)

**Tool Proficiency**: Mastery of the tools required to work efficiently (CRM, project management, data analysis, communication platforms)

**Process Knowledge**: Understanding of how work flows through the function — what happens, in what order, and why

**Relational Capabilities**: The ability to work effectively with customers, stakeholders, and other teams (not the same as "soft skills" — these are learnable and coachable)

**Capability Gap Identification:**
For each capability the team needs:
1. Rate the team's current proficiency (1–5 scale)
2. Identify the required proficiency level for the team's goals
3. Gap = Required − Current
4. For gaps: decide to **build** (training, development), **buy** (hire), or **borrow** (outsource, partner)

### A — Accountability: What the Team Owns

Accountability defines the outcomes the team is responsible for delivering and the metrics by which success is measured. Without explicit accountability, teams work hard on activities while remaining unclear on results.

Accountability operates at three levels in the High-Output Team Canvas:

**Team-Level Accountability**: The 3–5 outcomes the team as a whole is measured on. These appear on the team's scorecard and are reviewed weekly. The team lead owns these metrics collectively.

**Role-Level Accountability**: The specific outcomes each role is responsible for. Not activities ("the Account Executive prospects for new customers") but results ("the Account Executive is accountable for $500K ARR new business per quarter").

**Individual Accountability**: The specific deliverables and metrics each team member is measured on. These inform performance reviews, compensation, and development conversations.

**Defining Accountability: The Output, Not the Activity**

The single most common accountability design failure is defining roles by activities rather than outputs. A job description that reads "responsible for managing customer relationships" creates a role without accountability. One that reads "responsible for achieving Net Revenue Retention ≥ 115% for a portfolio of 50 accounts" creates clarity and measurability.

To identify real accountability:
1. Start with the outputs the business needs this function to produce
2. Work backwards to define which roles produce which outputs
3. Assign specific measurable targets to each output

**The Accountability Matrix:**

For each team, document:

| Role | Primary Output | Metric | Target | Review Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CSM | Customer retention and expansion | NRR per portfolio | ≥ 115% | Monthly |
| AE | New business revenue | New ARR | $500K/quarter | Weekly |
| SDR | Qualified pipeline | SQLs delivered | 20/week | Weekly |
| Customer Success Lead | Team NRR and capacity | Team NRR / utilization | ≥ 112% / ≤ 85% | Monthly |

### B — Behaviors: How the Team Acts

Behaviors are the specific ways the team is expected to work — not values or principles (those live in the Clarity Compass), but the behavioral standards that translate values into daily action within this specific team.

Behaviors are the most neglected component of team design, and the most consequential for culture. A team with strong capabilities and clear accountability but undefined behaviors will produce good work inconsistently, create internal friction, and struggle to onboard new members.

**The Behavior Standard Test:**
A behavior standard is useful only if:
1. A manager could observe whether someone is demonstrating it
2. Failing to demonstrate it would justify a coaching conversation
3. Consistently demonstrating it would be a reason to promote or recognize someone

**Behavioral standards that pass this test:**

*Vague (fails):* "We communicate openly."
*Specific (passes):* "We surface bad news within 24 hours of knowing it — we never let a problem fester to protect someone's comfort."

*Vague (fails):* "We are customer-focused."
*Specific (passes):* "Before sending a proposal, we confirm in writing with the customer what success looks like for them in 90 days."

*Vague (fails):* "We work collaboratively."
*Specific (passes):* "Before escalating a problem to another team, we spend 30 minutes attempting to solve it independently and document what we tried."

**Number of Behavioral Standards:**
3–5 per team. Fewer than 3 provides insufficient definition. More than 5 becomes unenforceable. The discipline of choosing fewer forces the team to agree on what matters most.

## Role Design Principles

### Define Roles by Outputs, Not Activities

Traditional job descriptions define roles by what people do. High-Output Team design defines roles by what they produce.

The difference:
- **Activity-based**: "The Marketing Manager creates and manages content across social media channels, coordinates with the design team, and writes email campaigns."
- **Output-based**: "The Marketing Manager is accountable for 3,000 MQLs per quarter and average CAC under $150, through any channel mix that achieves these outcomes."

Output-based role design enables autonomy (people can choose HOW to achieve their output), creates clear accountability, and makes performance management straightforward.

### Single-Threaded Ownership

For critical outcomes, one person owns the outcome end-to-end. Not "the revenue team" — one sales leader owns net new ARR. Not "the customer team" — one CS leader owns retention. Single-threaded ownership eliminates the diffusion of accountability that comes from shared responsibility.

Jeff Bezos called this the "single-threaded leader" model: one person is laser-focused on one thing and has the authority and resources to make it happen. This is the organizational design principle behind Amazon's team structure.

### Decision Rights

Each role must have clarity on what decisions it can make independently, what decisions require consultation, and what decisions require approval. Without this, teams either bottleneck decisions to the top (every decision goes to the manager) or make unauthorized decisions that cause expensive downstream problems.

**The Decision Rights Spectrum:**
- **Autonomous**: Team member decides and acts; informs manager afterward
- **Informed**: Team member decides and acts; informs manager before
- **Consultative**: Team member decides after consultation with manager or peers
- **Approval**: Manager decides; team member recommends
- **Delegated to manager**: Manager decides without team member input

Mapping decision rights for each role prevents both bottlenecking and unauthorized action.

## Team Structure Principles

### Team Size: The Two-Pizza Rule

Amazon's famous "two-pizza rule" is directional: if a team can't be fed by two large pizzas, it's too big. The practical implication: 5–8 people is the optimal team size for high coordination and clear accountability. At this size, communication overhead is manageable, each person's contribution is visible, and the team lead can maintain meaningful 1:1 relationships with each member.

Above 10 people, teams lose cohesion without structural intervention (sub-teams, pods, squads). Below 3 people, the team lacks resilience and diversity of perspective.

### Span of Control

Span of control is the number of direct reports a manager effectively manages. Too many direct reports and managers cannot provide adequate coaching, context, and feedback. Too few and the organization becomes top-heavy with management overhead.

**Rule of thumb:** 1 manager per 5–8 direct reports
- **Simpler, more routine work**: 6–10 is sustainable (factory operations, customer support)
- **Complex, creative, or high-autonomy work**: 4–6 is appropriate (product management, strategy, enterprise sales)
- **Early-stage or high-uncertainty roles**: 3–5 (new function, new market, significant coaching required)

When managers have too many direct reports, the warning signs are:
- 1:1s are cancelled or superficial
- Performance problems aren't caught early
- New hires take too long to onboard and reach full productivity
- Managers are executing, not managing

### Functional vs. Cross-Functional Teams

**Functional teams** (all marketers together, all engineers together) optimize for deep expertise and career development within a function. They work well when functions operate sequentially and handoffs are clear.

**Cross-functional teams** (product + engineering + design, or sales + CS + solutions) optimize for end-to-end ownership of a customer or outcome. They work well when the key bottleneck is handoffs and coordination between functions.

The right structure depends on the current constraint. If the biggest problems are quality within a function (engineering quality, sales quality), functional teams are appropriate. If the biggest problems are between functions (engineering builds things sales can't sell, CS inherits accounts that sales over-promised), cross-functional teams are appropriate.

## Building a High-Output Team: The Six-Step Process

### Step 1: Define the Team's Purpose

Before designing the team, articulate in one sentence what the team exists to produce. Not what activities the team will perform — what outcomes the business needs from this team.

"The Customer Success team exists to ensure that every customer achieves a meaningful outcome from [product] within 90 days of purchase and continues to find expanding value thereafter."

### Step 2: Map the Required Outputs

List every output the team needs to produce to fulfill its purpose. Keep the list to 5–8 outputs. For each output, define the metric that measures it and the quality standard that defines success.

### Step 3: Design the Roles

Working backwards from the outputs, define the roles needed to produce them. For each role:
- What is the primary output?
- What capability is required to produce it?
- What decisions does this role have authority to make?
- How will this role be measured?

### Step 4: Assess Current vs. Required Capabilities

For each role currently filled, assess the gap between the capabilities the role requires and the capabilities the current role-holder has. This is an honest assessment, not a performance review — it's a design gap analysis.

Gaps are addressed through:
- **Training / Development**: If the gap is bridgeable in 3–6 months with coaching and structured learning
- **Hiring**: If the gap requires expertise the company doesn't have and can't develop fast enough
- **Restructuring**: If the gap reveals that the role design is wrong (wrong scope, wrong level, wrong combination of outputs)

### Step 5: Set Behavioral Standards

With the team or team lead, define 3–5 specific behavioral standards for this team. Test each one: Can a manager observe it? Is it justification for a coaching conversation if absent? Is it worth recognizing when exemplified?

### Step 6: Build the Accountability System

Create the team scorecard (3–5 metrics), define review frequency, and assign one owner per metric. Connect team scorecard metrics to the company scorecard if applicable.

## High-Output Team and OKRs

The High-Output Team Canvas and the OKR framework work together. The Canvas defines the team structure and accountability. OKRs define the quarterly priorities *within* that structure.

The connection:
- **Team Accountability** (from Canvas) = the baseline outcomes the team must deliver every quarter just to maintain health
- **Team OKRs** = the stretch ambitions the team is chasing this quarter above and beyond the baseline

A CS team that maintains NRR ≥ 110% is performing to their accountability. A CS team whose Q3 OKR is "Drive NRR from 110% to 120% through a new expansion motion" is working above the baseline toward a stretch goal. Both need to be tracked, but they belong in different systems.
