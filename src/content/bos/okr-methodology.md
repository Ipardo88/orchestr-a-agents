# OKR Methodology — Measure What Matters

## What Are OKRs and Why They Work

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are a goal-setting framework that connects ambitious qualitative aspirations (Objectives) to specific, measurable outcomes (Key Results). Developed at Intel by Andy Grove in the 1970s and later popularized by John Doerr at Google — documented in his book *Measure What Matters* (2018) — OKRs have become the dominant strategic execution framework for high-growth companies.

The core insight behind OKRs is that **focus and alignment are the primary constraints on execution**, not talent or effort. Most organizations fail not because they lack capable people, but because those people are working on the wrong things, or working on the right things in ways that don't reinforce each other. OKRs solve this by making priorities explicit, visible, and cascaded.

OKRs work because they encode four operational principles:

1. **Focus** — Forcing a small number of objectives ensures the organization concentrates energy rather than diffusing it.
2. **Alignment** — Publishing OKRs at every level makes dependencies and overlaps visible, so teams coordinate rather than collide.
3. **Commitment and accountability** — Named owners and measurable key results create unambiguous accountability.
4. **Stretch** — OKRs are designed to be ambitious enough that 70% achievement is considered a success. This prevents sandbagging and encourages moonshot thinking.

The framework gained adoption at Google (1999), LinkedIn, Twitter, Uber, Airbnb, and Spotify — all of which attribute a significant portion of their scaling success to OKR discipline. In the B2B SaaS context specifically, OKRs are the connective tissue between a company's strategic intent (the "What to Play" and "How to Win" choices from the Playing to Win framework) and the day-to-day work of product, sales, and customer success teams.

## The OKR Hierarchy: Company → BU → Team → Individual Cascade

OKRs operate across four levels of organizational scope:

### Level 1: Company OKRs
Set by the CEO and leadership team, typically 3–5 objectives covering the 12–18 month strategic horizon broken into quarterly increments. Company OKRs should directly reflect the strategic goals and the "How to Win" choices. If there is a strategic goal to "dominate the mid-market segment in EMEA," the company OKR should make that concrete: "Become the market-share leader in EMEA mid-market SaaS security tools."

### Level 2: Business Unit (BU) OKRs
Each BU or function takes the company OKRs and asks: "What is our specific contribution to this?" Sales has different levers than Engineering; Customer Success has different levers than Marketing. BU OKRs are translations, not copies.

- Company OKR: "Achieve ARR of $50M by end of year"
- Sales BU OKR: "Build a pipeline machine that delivers $20M of net new ARR in H2"
- CS BU OKR: "Drive NRR from 108% to 120% through expansion and churn reduction"

### Level 3: Team OKRs
Teams within each BU define how their specific work contributes to the BU objective. Teams are usually 5–10 people with a specific product area, market segment, or function (e.g., Enterprise AE team, Onboarding squad, Data platform team).

- CS BU OKR: "Drive NRR from 108% to 120%"
- Onboarding Team OKR: "Eliminate the onboarding gap that causes 60-day churn — achieve time-to-first-value under 7 days for all new accounts"

### Level 4: Individual OKRs
Not all organizations use individual OKRs, and there is healthy debate about whether they should be tied to performance reviews (Doerr explicitly recommends against this). When used, individual OKRs should be owned by the individual and connect to their team OKR, not imposed top-down.

### The Cascade Test
After all OKRs are set, apply this test: "If every team achieves its OKRs, will the company OKR be achieved?" If the answer is no, the cascade is broken. Common cascade breaks include:

- A company OKR for revenue growth with no corresponding sales team OKR for pipeline
- A company OKR for customer retention with no CS team OKR addressing the root cause of churn
- A product OKR for feature velocity with no link to which customer problem it solves

## Objective Design Principles: Qualitative, Ambitious, Inspirational

A well-designed Objective has three non-negotiable properties:

### 1. Qualitative
Objectives should NOT contain numbers. Numbers belong in Key Results. An objective with a number becomes a KPI, not an OKR. The qualitative nature of the objective is what makes it inspirational and context-setting.

- **Weak (contains number):** "Grow ARR to $30M this quarter"
- **Strong (qualitative):** "Make this the quarter we prove our enterprise motion works"

### 2. Ambitious (Stretch Principle)
An objective that is easily achievable is not an OKR — it is a task. John Doerr and Google's Rick Klau advocate for "stretch OKRs" where the intended achievement is 70% of the stated objective. If you achieve 100%, the goal was too easy.

The practical implication: leadership must psychologically accept that not all OKRs will be fully achieved, and that partial achievement of a stretch goal is more valuable than full achievement of a sandbagged goal.

### 3. Inspirational
People should be able to read an objective and feel a sense of purpose. "Become the platform that defines how mid-market companies do strategic planning" is more motivating than "capture market share in the SMB segment."

### Objective Vocabulary Patterns That Work
- "Become the [superlative] in [domain]"
- "Prove that we can [capability] at scale"
- "Make [customer type] wildly successful through [mechanism]"
- "Turn [pain point] into our biggest competitive advantage"
- "Build the foundation for [future state]"

### Common Objective Anti-Patterns
- "Improve X" — vague, not inspirational
- "Continue doing X" — no ambition
- "Deliver X initiative" — output, not outcome
- "Achieve [number]" — metric, not objective

## Key Result Design: 2–4 Per Objective, Measurable, Time-Bound, Specific

Key Results are the quantitative evidence that an Objective has been achieved. They are not tasks, not activities, and not projects — they are outcome measurements.

### The SMART-Plus Test for Key Results
Every KR should pass:
- **Specific**: It measures exactly one thing, clearly defined
- **Measurable**: It has a numerical value (percentage, count, currency, ratio, score)
- **Achievable**: Ambitious but not impossible
- **Relevant**: Achieving this KR genuinely indicates the Objective is being met
- **Time-bound**: It has a deadline (usually end of quarter)
- **Baseline**: It starts from a known current value

### 2–4 Key Results Per Objective
Too few KRs and the objective can be gamed (optimize one metric at the expense of everything else). Too many KRs and the objective becomes a laundry list. 3 KRs is the sweet spot in most cases:

- KR1: Primary outcome metric (what does success look like?)
- KR2: Leading indicator or quality guardrail (are we getting there the right way?)
- KR3: Counter-metric or coverage metric (are we avoiding unintended consequences?)

### Example: Enterprise Sales Motion OKR

**Objective:** Make this the quarter we prove our enterprise motion can scale

- KR1: Close 5 net-new enterprise logos (ACV > $80K) — from 1 in Q2
- KR2: Achieve average sales cycle under 90 days for enterprise deals
- KR3: Enterprise pipeline coverage reaches 4x of target by end of month 2

### Example: Customer Retention OKR

**Objective:** Turn customer success into our most powerful growth lever

- KR1: Increase NRR from 107% to 118%
- KR2: Reduce 90-day churn from 8% to 3% of new cohorts
- KR3: Achieve onboarding NPS ≥ 55 (baseline: 32)
- KR4: Expand revenue from existing customers represents ≥ 40% of total new ARR

### Example: Product Capability OKR

**Objective:** Become the platform mid-market strategists trust above all others

- KR1: 3 new strategic analysis modules shipped and adopted by ≥ 60% of active users within 30 days of launch
- KR2: AI-powered recommendations feature achieves ≥ 70% positive user rating (thumbs up / thumbs down)
- KR3: Weekly active usage increases from 2.1 to 3.8 sessions per user

## The OKR Cycle: Quarterly Rhythm

The standard OKR cadence is quarterly, with an annual planning layer on top.

### Annual Planning (Q4, 4–6 weeks before year-end)
- Define company-level annual OKRs or themes
- Set Q1 OKRs for company and BU level
- Communicate strategic direction

### Quarterly Kickoff (Week 1 of quarter)
- Finalize and publish OKRs at all levels
- Alignment check: does the cascade hold?
- Assign owners at every level
- Set baseline values for all KRs

### Weekly: KR Updates
- Each KR owner updates `current_value` in the tracking system
- Status automatically computed: on_track / at_risk / behind
- No meeting required for green status; flags trigger review

### Monthly: Mid-Cycle Check-In (End of Month 1 and Month 2)
- Leadership reviews at-risk and behind OKRs
- Decide: adjust scope, reallocate resources, or accept the miss
- Document the decision and rationale
- Update KR trajectories

### End-of-Quarter: Scoring and Retrospective
Score each KR on a 0.0–1.0 scale:
- **1.0**: Fully achieved (rare and signals the goal was sandbagged)
- **0.7**: Strong achievement — the intended "full success" for stretch goals
- **0.5–0.6**: Partially achieved — significant progress but fell short
- **0.3–0.4**: Minimal progress — intervention needed
- **0.0**: No meaningful progress — investigate root cause

Then conduct a qualitative retrospective:
1. What did we learn about our strategy?
2. Were the OKRs the right ones to focus on?
3. What blocked us that we should remove next quarter?
4. Which OKRs should continue, evolve, or be retired?

The retrospective output directly informs the next quarter's OKR-setting session.

## OKR Status Definitions

In the OrchestrA platform, status is computed automatically from progress relative to time elapsed:

| Status | Condition | Meaning |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| `on_track` | Progress ≥ 70% of expected pace | Healthy, on course |
| `at_risk` | Progress 40–70% of expected pace | Needs attention, may not reach target |
| `behind` | Progress < 40% of expected pace | Significant intervention required |
| `completed` | End date passed, KRs scored | Quarter closed |
| `paused` | Explicitly paused | Deprioritized mid-cycle |
| `draft` | Not yet started | Setup phase |

**Status Rollup Rule (Dominant Status):** When an objective has multiple KRs with different statuses, the most severe status propagates upward: `behind` > `at_risk` > `on_track`. Exception: if all KRs are `completed`, the objective shows `completed`.

**Portfolio Health Target:** A healthy OKR portfolio has ≥ 80% of objectives on track at any given mid-cycle check-in. If all OKRs are green, the goals are not ambitious enough (stretch principle). If more than 30% are behind at mid-quarter, there may be a capacity or planning issue to investigate.

## Alignment: Tracing Every OKR to the Level Above

Every OKR in the organization should be traceable to a higher-level OKR or strategic goal. In OrchestrA, this alignment is enforced through:

1. **Strategic Goal Linking**: Company-level objectives can be linked directly to a `strategic_goal` (from the strategic goals table), which ties them to the Playing to Win framework
2. **Parent-Child Hierarchy**: BU and team OKRs reference a `parent_id` pointing to the company or BU objective they support
3. **Cascade Visualization**: The OKR Alignment Map (React Flow canvas) visualizes the full cascade tree

### Alignment Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- **Orphaned OKRs**: Team OKRs with no parent — these are team pet projects, not strategic execution
- **Disconnected BU OKRs**: BU objectives that don't map to any company objective indicate misalignment in planning
- **Reverse alignment**: Creating company OKRs by aggregating team OKRs bottom-up (this is bottoms-up activity planning, not strategic alignment)

## Common OKR Pitfalls

### 1. Sandbagging
Setting targets you know you will achieve because failure looks bad. Sandbagging destroys the stretch principle and makes OKRs useless as a driver of ambitious behavior. Leadership must model psychological safety around partial achievement of stretch goals.

### 2. Too Many OKRs
3–5 objectives per level is the firm maximum. More than 5 means everything is a priority, which means nothing is a priority. When teams list 10+ OKRs, they are confusing their roadmap with their OKRs.

### 3. Output vs. Outcome Confusion
The most common mistake. "Launch feature X" is an output — it's what you do. "Increase daily active users by 20% through feature X" is an outcome — it's what changes as a result of what you do. KRs must be outcomes.

- **Output (bad)**: "Complete the onboarding redesign"
- **Outcome (good)**: "Reduce time-to-first-value from 45 to 7 days"

### 4. Missing Baselines
Every KR needs a start value. "Achieve NPS of 50" is ambiguous — is that from 20 or from 48? Without a baseline, scoring and trend analysis are impossible.

### 5. OKRs Disconnected from Strategy
If you cannot trace an OKR to a strategic goal or a "How to Win" choice, question whether it should exist. OKRs are for executing strategy, not for tracking operational tasks.

### 6. OKRs as Performance Reviews
When OKR achievement is directly tied to compensation or performance ratings, people sandbag to protect themselves. Doerr explicitly warns against this. OKRs are a coordination tool, not an HR evaluation tool.

### 7. Set-and-Forget
OKRs require ongoing attention. If teams only look at them at end of quarter, the framework provides no mid-cycle course correction value.

## OKR Design Checklist

Before publishing OKRs at any level:

- [ ] Objective is qualitative (no numbers) and genuinely ambitious
- [ ] 2–4 key results per objective (no more, no fewer)
- [ ] Each KR has: start value, target value, unit, and deadline
- [ ] Each objective has a named single owner (not a team)
- [ ] Each company-level objective links to a strategic goal
- [ ] Each BU/team OKR has a parent_id pointing to a higher-level OKR
- [ ] Cascade test passes: if all team OKRs are achieved, company OKRs are achieved
- [ ] No more than 5 objectives per level
- [ ] No KRs are activities or project milestones (outcomes only)
- [ ] Status threshold logic is confirmed (on_track ≥ 70%, at_risk 40–70%, behind < 40%)
