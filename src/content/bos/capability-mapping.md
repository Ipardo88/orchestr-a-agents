# Capability Mapping — Playing to Win

## What Is a Business Capability?

A capability is something a business must be able to **DO** to execute its strategy. Capabilities are not processes, tools, or people in isolation — they are the integrated combination of skills, systems, culture, knowledge, and organizational design that enables a specific type of value creation at a repeatable, high level of quality.

The key test: a capability is something a competitor would have to spend years and significant capital to replicate if they saw you doing it well.

**Examples of genuine capabilities:**
- "Customer segmentation and precision targeting" — combining data analytics, ICP definition expertise, and marketing execution into a repeatable competitive edge
- "Rapid product iteration (≤ 2-week cycle from insight to production)" — requiring engineering culture, CI/CD infrastructure, product judgment, and customer feedback loops
- "Channel partner ecosystem development" — requiring relationship management, partner enablement programs, incentive design, and joint go-to-market playbooks
- "Enterprise consultative selling" — requiring discovery methodology, executive access, solution design skills, and negotiation acumen
- "Real-time financial analytics and forecasting" — requiring data engineering, financial modeling expertise, and business partner relationships

**Capabilities vs. what they are NOT:**
- Not a process: "Our Salesforce CRM workflow" is a process. "Sales velocity optimization" is a capability.
- Not a tool: "We use Amplitude for analytics" is a tool. "Data-driven product decision making" is a capability.
- Not a department: "We have a 50-person engineering team" is a headcount. "Rapid, high-quality software delivery" is a capability.

## Capabilities vs. Activities: The Critical Distinction

Activities are what you do day-to-day. Capabilities are what you must be genuinely excellent at to win. This distinction matters because it determines where to invest.

| Day-to-Day Activity | Underlying Strategic Capability |
|---------------------|--------------------------------|
| Running discovery calls with prospects | Consultative enterprise selling |
| Shipping product features on a sprint cycle | Rapid product development and iteration |
| Onboarding new customers after contract sign | Low-friction, high-speed customer onboarding |
| Producing monthly financial reports | Real-time financial intelligence and forecasting |
| Running performance reviews | High-performance culture development |
| Responding to support tickets | Proactive customer success and churn prevention |
| Managing partner contracts | Channel ecosystem development and enablement |
| Training new sales hires | Scalable revenue team development |

The activity level is what managers see and manage. The capability level is what strategy must address. If your strategy requires "winning through superior customer experience" but you treat onboarding as a series of tasks rather than a capability to develop, the strategy will fail execution.

## The Playing to Win Framework Connection

Roger Martin's *Playing to Win* (2013) defines strategy as an integrated set of choices: Winning Aspiration, Where to Play, How to Win, and the Capabilities and Management Systems required to execute. Capabilities are the fifth element — they are not an afterthought but the operational heart of the strategy.

The logic flows as follows:

```
Winning Aspiration
       ↓
Where to Play (markets, segments, geographies, channels)
       ↓
How to Win (the competitive differentiator in those markets)
       ↓
CAPABILITIES (what you must do excellently to win that way)
       ↓
Management Systems (processes, incentives, structures that enable capabilities)
```

### The "How to Win" → Capability Link

The How to Win choice is a claim about *why* customers in your chosen markets will prefer you over competitors. Capabilities are the organizational realities that make that claim credible and defensible.

**Example 1: "Win through lowest total cost of ownership in the SMB segment"**
Required capabilities:
- Self-serve product onboarding (low implementation cost)
- Highly automated customer success (low support cost)
- Product-led growth (low CAC)
- Infrastructure efficiency (low COGS)

**Example 2: "Win through superior outcomes in complex enterprise deployments"**
Required capabilities:
- Consultative enterprise sales methodology
- Executive relationship management (C-suite access)
- Custom implementation and professional services
- Deep product configurability
- Enterprise-grade security and compliance

**Example 3: "Win by being the AI-powered insight layer for mid-market strategy teams"**
Required capabilities:
- AI/ML model development and productization
- Strategic frameworks knowledge and encoding
- User experience for non-technical strategy professionals
- Rapid insight generation from unstructured data

### The Capability Test
For each claimed capability, apply this test: *"If we had this capability at world-class level, would it materially advance our How to Win?"*

- If YES → it is a strategic capability that deserves investment
- If NO → it is table stakes (hygiene) or a distraction

## Critical vs. Supporting Capabilities

Not all capabilities deserve equal investment. The Playing to Win framework makes an explicit distinction:

### Critical Capabilities (Must Be World-Class)
2–4 capabilities that are the source of competitive advantage. These are the capabilities that, if your competitor developed them, would directly threaten your ability to win. They deserve **disproportionate investment** — the best people, the most budget, the most leadership attention.

Critical capabilities are:
- Directly linked to the How to Win choice
- Difficult to replicate (require time, culture, or proprietary knowledge)
- The reason customers choose you over alternatives

**B2B SaaS examples of critical capabilities:**
- **Product-led growth engine**: If your How to Win is "lowest friction adoption," your ability to drive viral, self-serve expansion is critical
- **Enterprise sales excellence**: If your How to Win is "win the Fortune 500 in EMEA," the quality of your enterprise sales process is critical
- **Data and AI platform**: If your How to Win is "best predictive insights," your ML infrastructure and data science capability is critical
- **Customer success at scale**: If your How to Win is "highest retention in the category," proactive CS is critical

### Supporting Capabilities (Table Stakes)
Capabilities you must have to operate but that don't differentiate you. Finance, basic HR, legal, IT infrastructure, standard security compliance. These should be **efficient**, not excellent. Over-investing in supporting capabilities is a misallocation of strategic resources.

**Examples:**
- Financial reporting and audit compliance → sufficient, not excellent
- Employee payroll and benefits administration → outsource or use standard tools
- Basic IT helpdesk → automate where possible
- Standard security certifications (SOC2, ISO) → necessary but not differentiating

**Design principle**: For supporting capabilities, aim for the lowest-cost, highest-reliability solution. For critical capabilities, invest until you reach world-class, then defend that position aggressively.

## Capability Assessment Framework

For each critical capability, assess two dimensions to produce a gap score:

### Dimension 1: Current State (0–10)
How effective is this capability today?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0–2 | This capability effectively doesn't exist. We are starting from scratch. |
| 3–4 | Early stage. We have some people or processes in place but execution is inconsistent. |
| 5–6 | Developing. We can execute reasonably well in favorable conditions but struggle with complexity or scale. |
| 7–8 | Competent. We execute this consistently well. We beat most competitors on this dimension. |
| 9–10 | World-class. Recognized as excellent internally and externally. This is a genuine competitive moat. |

### Dimension 2: Required State (0–10)
How good does this capability need to be to execute the strategy and achieve the How to Win?

This is set by the strategic ambition, not by comparison to competitors. If the How to Win requires world-class onboarding (10), but you currently rate it a 4, the gap is 6 — regardless of what competitors do.

### Gap Calculation
**Gap = Required State − Current State**

| Gap | Interpretation |
|-----|----------------|
| 0–1 | No material gap. Monitor and maintain. |
| 2–3 | Small gap. Address through continuous improvement in normal operations. |
| 4–5 | Significant gap. Requires a dedicated OKR and budget allocation. |
| 6–7 | Strategic risk. This gap is threatening the ability to execute the strategy. Requires immediate prioritization. |
| 8+ | Existential threat to the strategy. May require a strategic acquisition, major hiring program, or partnership to address in time. |

### Assessment Example: B2B SaaS Strategy Platform

| Capability | Current (0–10) | Required (0–10) | Gap | Priority |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|-----|----------|
| AI-powered insight generation | 3 | 9 | 6 | Critical — strategic risk |
| Strategic frameworks encoding | 6 | 9 | 3 | Significant — needs OKR |
| Product-led onboarding | 4 | 8 | 4 | Significant — needs OKR |
| Enterprise sales methodology | 5 | 7 | 2 | Small — continuous improvement |
| Data integration ecosystem | 2 | 8 | 6 | Critical — strategic risk |
| Financial reporting accuracy | 7 | 7 | 0 | Table stakes — maintain |

## Building a Capability Development Roadmap

A capability roadmap translates gap scores into a sequenced investment plan. It answers: "In what order do we close which gaps, and with what resources?"

### Sequencing Principles

**1. Address existential gaps first**
Gaps ≥ 6 in capabilities that are directly required for the current How to Win should be treated as strategic emergencies. They cannot wait for the annual planning cycle.

**2. Sequence for compounding**
Some capabilities are prerequisites for others. You cannot build "enterprise consultative selling" without first building "ICP definition and targeting." Map the dependency chain before sequencing.

**3. Match investment to gap severity**
A gap of 2–3 can be addressed through internal development, coaching, and process improvement. A gap of 6+ may require external hiring, acquisitions, or partnerships. Don't try to close a gap of 7 purely through internal L&D.

**4. One or two major capability gaps per quarter**
The organization can focus on developing at most 1–2 capabilities per quarter. Trying to close 5 gaps simultaneously diffuses effort and closes none.

### Roadmap Example (B2B SaaS, 4-Quarter View)

**Q1: Foundation — Data Integration Ecosystem (Gap: 6)**
- Hire VP of Partnerships and 2 integration engineers
- Build first 5 native integrations with top tools in ICP's stack
- OKR: Reduce integration setup time from 2 weeks to 2 days

**Q2: AI Differentiation — AI-Powered Insight Generation (Gap: 6)**
- Staff ML team (3 ML engineers, 1 data scientist)
- Release first AI-driven recommendation feature to beta users
- OKR: AI insights feature achieves ≥ 65% positive rating from beta users

**Q3: Go-to-Market — Product-Led Onboarding (Gap: 4)**
- Redesign onboarding flow with PLG principles (no sales touch for < 50-seat accounts)
- OKR: Time to first value from 45 days to 7 days for SMB segment

**Q4: Scale — Strategic Frameworks Encoding (Gap: 3)**
- Partner with 2 strategy consultancies to encode frameworks into the AI layer
- OKR: Platform covers 8 of 10 most-used strategy frameworks with AI-guided exercises

## How Capabilities Connect to People Development

Capabilities are built through people. Every capability has a workforce implication — the skills, experience, and organizational design needed to execute it.

### Capability → Skills Matrix Connection
For each critical capability, define:
1. **Core skills required**: What knowledge and skill domains must people have?
2. **Current skills inventory**: What do we have today (from the skills matrix)?
3. **Skill gap**: Which skills are missing or insufficient?
4. **Development path**: Hire, train, or partner?

**Example: Building "Enterprise Consultative Sales" Capability**

Required skills:
- Executive storytelling and C-suite communication (Level: Advanced)
- Value engineering and ROI modeling (Level: Proficient)
- Complex negotiation and deal structuring (Level: Advanced)
- CRM hygiene and pipeline management discipline (Level: Proficient)
- Strategic account planning (Level: Advanced)

Skills gap assessment:
- Executive storytelling: Current average Level 2/4 → gap of 2 levels → training program
- Value engineering: Nobody on team has this → hire 1 SE with this skill
- Negotiation: 2 of 8 reps are Advanced → coaching program for remaining 6

### Hiring vs. Training vs. Partnership Decision

| Gap Type | Recommended Approach |
|----------|---------------------|
| Skill exists at junior level, needs development | Training + coaching + stretch assignments |
| Skill exists in one or two people, needs scaling | Hire to fill coverage gaps; internal train |
| Skill doesn't exist in the organization | External hire or partner with firms that have it |
| Capability is commoditizing or not differentiating | Outsource or use best-in-class SaaS tools |
| Time pressure is extreme (gap threatens this quarter) | Acquire or bring in consultants while building internal |

## The Capability-OKR Link

Every critical capability gap should have an OKR attached to it. If a capability gap exists with no OKR or KPI, the business is not investing in its own strategy — it has identified a problem and chosen to do nothing about it.

### Capability OKR Pattern

**Objective**: "[Capability] must reach the level we need to win in [timeframe]"

Key Results are a combination of:
- The capability metric itself (current state score improvement)
- A measurable output that proves the capability improved
- A business outcome that validates the capability is working

**Example: Capability OKR for "AI-Powered Insight Generation"**

**Objective**: Build AI insight generation to the level where it becomes our primary product differentiator this year

- KR1: AI recommendation accuracy rating reaches ≥ 75% (user thumbs up/down) — from baseline of 41%
- KR2: Time from data input to AI-generated strategic insight < 2 minutes for 90% of use cases
- KR3: 60% of active platform users engage with AI features at least 3x per week
- KR4: Win rate in competitive deals where AI features were demonstrated reaches ≥ 45%

Note how KR1 and KR2 measure the capability directly, while KR3 and KR4 measure the business impact of the capability — proving it actually matters to customers.

### KPI for Ongoing Capability Health
Once a capability gap is closed to an acceptable level, it should transition from an OKR to a KPI:

- Capability OKR (closes the gap): "Build onboarding capability — reduce TTFV from 45 to 7 days"
- Capability KPI (monitors steady state): "Time to First Value" — Target: < 7 days, Amber: > 14 days, Red: > 30 days

## Common Capability Mapping Mistakes

### 1. Listing Activities as Capabilities
"Running weekly team meetings" is not a capability. "Cross-functional collaboration and rapid decision making" is a capability. Activities are what you do; capabilities are what you are able to do at a high level, repeatedly.

Test: Can a new VP of Sales copy your "capability" by reading a process document in a week? If yes, it's not a strategic capability — it's a process.

### 2. Too Many "Critical" Capabilities
If everything is critical, nothing is. Most businesses can only develop 2–4 genuinely world-class capabilities at a time. Listing 10 critical capabilities is a symptom of failure to make strategic choices.

Ask leadership: "If we had to invest all our capability development budget in just 2 capabilities, which 2 would make us most likely to win?" That forces the real prioritization conversation.

### 3. Capabilities Disconnected from Strategy
A capability assessment that is not derived from the How to Win choice is just an HR exercise. Start always from "How to Win" and work backward to "Therefore, we must be excellent at…"

If a capability doesn't trace to a specific How to Win choice, it should be reclassified as table stakes or removed from the critical list.

### 4. No Gap Assessment
Mapping capabilities without scoring current and required state is "decoration, not strategy" (Roger Martin). Without a gap score, there is no prioritization, no investment case, and no OKR to close the gap.

Every capability assessment must result in a 0–10 current state score and 0–10 required state score. No score = no accountability.

### 5. No OKR for Capability Gaps ≥ 4
A gap with no action plan will not close. If a strategic capability has a gap of 4 or more and there is no OKR assigned to it this quarter, the organization is accepting strategic underperformance without a plan to change it.

Build the rule: "Every capability gap ≥ 4 must have an OKR owner and a budget line. Every capability gap ≥ 6 must be reviewed by the CEO."

### 6. One-Time Assessment, No Refresh
Capability assessments should be refreshed at minimum annually (ideally at each quarterly strategy review). Capabilities can erode (through attrition) or improve (through investment). A capability map that is 18 months old is not a strategy tool — it's a historical document.

## Capability Mapping Process: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Start from How to Win
Pull the confirmed "How to Win" statement from the strategic intent module. This is the anchor for everything that follows.

### Step 2: Identify Required Capabilities
Ask the leadership team: "To win this way, what must we be genuinely excellent at?" Generate a list of 6–10 candidates using brainstorming or structured workshops.

Prompt questions to generate the list:
- "What would a world-class competitor do that we can't currently match?"
- "What capability, if we built it, would make this strategy nearly unbeatable?"
- "Where are customers choosing alternatives, and what capability would change that?"
- "What capability does our best existing customer value most about working with us?"

### Step 3: Classify Critical vs. Supporting
Vote on the candidate capabilities: Which are critical to the How to Win (would provide genuine competitive advantage if world-class), and which are table stakes (you need them but they don't differentiate)?

Aim for 2–4 critical, rest as supporting.

### Step 4: Assess Current State (0–10)
For each critical capability, conduct a structured assessment. Include multiple voices: product, sales, CS, engineering, and customer research if available. Avoid over-relying on self-assessment — the team building the capability tends to rate it higher than customers experience it.

### Step 5: Define Required State (0–10)
Set by the strategic ambition: "To fully execute the How to Win, this capability needs to be at level X." This should be set top-down, not benchmarked to competitors (benchmarking leads to "be as good as competitors," not "be distinctively better").

### Step 6: Calculate Gaps and Prioritize
Compute Gap = Required − Current for each critical capability. Sort by gap size descending. Top-priority gaps (≥ 6) go into immediate OKR planning.

### Step 7: Create OKRs for Significant Gaps
For each gap ≥ 4, create an OKR with:
- Objective: The capability improvement ambition
- Key Results: Measurable evidence the capability has improved
- Owner: A single named leader accountable for the capability
- Resources: Budget, headcount, and tools allocated

### Step 8: Build the Roadmap
Sequence capability development across 2–4 quarters. Account for dependencies (some capabilities must come before others), organizational capacity (max 2 capability development pushes per quarter), and urgency (existential gaps must be addressed first).

### Step 9: Review and Refresh
At each quarterly strategy review: re-assess current state scores to track progress, update gaps, revise OKRs as capabilities develop, and reconsider whether the required state has changed (market shifts can change what "world-class" means).
