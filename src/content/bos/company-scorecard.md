# Company Scorecard — Measuring What Drives the Business

## What Is the Company Scorecard

The Company Scorecard is the weekly pulse check on business health. It is the mechanism that converts strategy and OKRs — which live at quarterly and annual timescales — into a weekly discipline of awareness, accountability, and course correction.

Ryan Deiss's *Get Scalable* defines the Company Scorecard as one of the five core components of the Scalable Operating System (SOS). Its purpose is deceptively simple: make the health of the business visible to everyone who needs to act on it, at the cadence that enables action.

The Scorecard operates on three levels:
1. **Company Scorecard**: The 8–12 metrics that tell leadership whether the business is healthy
2. **Functional / Team Scorecards**: The 3–5 metrics each team owns and reports weekly
3. **North Star Metric**: The one metric that best captures the value the business delivers

Without a Scorecard, leadership teams manage by feeling — they know things are going well or badly before they can measure why, and by the time they can measure why it's too late to course-correct easily. With a Scorecard, problems surface within a week of appearing, and the conversation moves from "how do we feel about last quarter?" to "what do we do this week?"

## The Three-Layer Scorecard Architecture

### Layer 1: The Company Scorecard (Board-Level View)

The Company Scorecard contains 8–12 metrics that the leadership team reviews every week. These are the vital signs of the business — the metrics that, if any one of them went to red, would require immediate leadership attention.

The Company Scorecard is not a dashboard of every metric the business tracks. It is a curated selection of the metrics that matter most for answering: *Is the business healthy this week?*

**Design principles for the Company Scorecard:**
- **Weekly readability**: Every metric on the scorecard must have a weekly data point. If you can only get this number monthly, it belongs in the monthly review, not the weekly scorecard.
- **Actionability**: Every metric should trigger an action if it turns red. If a metric going red wouldn't change what anyone does, it doesn't belong on the scorecard.
- **Coverage across Value Engines**: The scorecard should include metrics from the Growth Engine, Fulfillment Engine, and financial health. A scorecard of only revenue metrics misses the early warning signals in fulfillment.
- **Mix of leading and lagging**: Revenue this week is a lagging indicator. Pipeline entered this week is a leading indicator. A healthy scorecard has both.

### Layer 2: Team Scorecards

Each team (Sales, Marketing, Customer Success, Engineering, Operations, Finance) maintains a scorecard of 3–5 metrics that they own and report weekly. These metrics roll up to inform the Company Scorecard.

Team scorecards serve a different function than the company scorecard: they create individual and team accountability at the functional level. A CS team that knows their retention rate will appear on the weekly scorecard — owned by them, visible to leadership — operates differently than a CS team that reports quarterly.

The golden rule of team scorecards: **each metric on a team scorecard must have a named owner**. Not "the CS team owns churn." "Sarah owns gross churn; Marcus owns NRR." One name per metric.

### Layer 3: The North Star Metric

The North Star Metric (NSM) is the single metric that best represents the value the business delivers to customers. It is not a financial metric — revenue, margin, and cash flow are measures of what the business extracts from value delivery, not the value delivery itself. The North Star Metric measures customer success.

Ryan Deiss and the broader product strategy community (including Sean Ellis and the growth hacking movement) converge on this concept: every business has one metric that, if it's improving, the business is genuinely getting better for customers. When the North Star Metric improves, everything else tends to follow. When it declines while revenue is growing, the business is extracting short-term value at the cost of long-term health.

**Examples by business model:**
| Business Model | Potential North Star Metrics |
|---|---|
| B2B SaaS | Daily/Weekly Active Users, Feature Adoption Rate, Outcomes Achieved |
| Professional Services | Client Outcomes Achieved, NPS, Renewal Rate |
| E-commerce | Repeat Purchase Rate, Lifetime Value, Products Per Customer |
| Marketplace | Successful Transactions, Buyer-Seller Match Rate |
| B2B Consulting | Strategic Goals Met, Decision Adoption Rate |

The North Star Metric is tracked on the company scorecard and reviewed every week. Its long-term trend — not week-to-week fluctuation — is the signal that matters most.

## Designing a Great Company Scorecard

### Step 1: Identify the 12 Candidate Metrics

Start with a list of all metrics the business currently tracks. Then ask of each: "If this metric went to red this week, would leadership take a different action?" If yes, it's a candidate. If no, it's a monitoring metric (tracked but not on the weekly scorecard).

Typical candidates by category:

**Growth Engine:**
- New leads / MQLs this week
- Pipeline created this week
- Demos scheduled
- Win rate (rolling 4-week average)
- New customers signed

**Fulfillment Engine:**
- Customer NPS (rolling or this month's cohort)
- Time-to-first-value (new customer average)
- Gross churn rate (monthly)
- Net Revenue Retention (monthly)
- Open support tickets / escalations

**Financial Health:**
- Revenue (this week / month-to-date)
- Gross margin (month-to-date)
- Cash and cash burn
- Accounts receivable > 30 days

**Team / People:**
- Employee NPS or pulse score
- Open roles (time-to-fill is a common addition)

### Step 2: Curate to 8–12 Metrics

From the candidates, select 8–12 that cover all three Value Engines and provide both leading and lagging indicators. Resist the temptation to include more — every additional metric reduces the attention paid to each.

A common first draft of the Company Scorecard for a B2B SaaS business at $5M–$30M ARR:

| # | Metric | Owner | Target | Red Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | New MQLs | Head of Marketing | 50/week | < 35 |
| 2 | Pipeline Created ($) | Head of Sales | $500K/week | < $300K |
| 3 | Deals Closed | Head of Sales | 5/week | < 3 |
| 4 | ARR (MTD) | CFO | On pace | 10% behind pace |
| 5 | Net New ARR | CFO | $200K/week | < $120K |
| 6 | Customer NPS | Head of CS | ≥ 50 | < 40 |
| 7 | Gross Churn | Head of CS | < 1.5%/month | > 2.5% |
| 8 | NRR (rolling 3-month) | Head of CS | > 110% | < 100% |
| 9 | Cash on Hand | CFO | > 6-month runway | < 3-month runway |
| 10 | Employee NPS | Head of People | ≥ 30 | < 20 |

### Step 3: Set Thresholds for Every Metric

Each metric needs three zones:
- **Green** (On track): Target is achievable, no action needed
- **Yellow / Amber** (At risk): Below target, monitor closely and identify cause
- **Red** (Off track): Requires immediate leadership attention and intervention

Thresholds are not arbitrary — they should reflect the level at which, if sustained for 4 weeks, the business would be materially harmed.

### Step 4: Assign One Owner Per Metric

Every metric has exactly one owner. Not a team. Not a "joint ownership." One name. This person is responsible for:
- Updating the metric before the weekly scorecard meeting
- Understanding the root cause when it turns yellow or red
- Coming to the meeting with a proposed explanation and, for red metrics, a proposed response

## The Weekly Scorecard Meeting

### Structure and Cadence

The weekly scorecard meeting is the heartbeat of the BOS. It is 60–90 minutes, held at the same time every week, and follows a consistent agenda. Consistency is what gives it power — when every leader knows the meeting format, they prepare differently.

**Standard 60-Minute Scorecard Meeting Agenda:**

| Time | Segment | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Wins and Announcements | Quick positive framing; keep brief |
| 5–35 min | Scorecard Review | Every metric reviewed; owners report status |
| 35–55 min | Blockers and Issues | Anything yellow/red gets root-cause discussion |
| 55–60 min | Action Items | One owner per action; deadline assigned |

### Rules for an Effective Scorecard Meeting

**Review every metric, every week.** Green metrics get 20 seconds ("NRR is at 115%, green, no issues"). Yellow and red metrics get 5–10 minutes. Skipping metrics "because they're fine" erodes the discipline.

**The owner speaks first.** When a metric is yellow or red, the owner speaks before anyone else offers a diagnosis. This prevents the meeting from becoming a forum where everyone speculates about why a number is down except the person who knows.

**Solve in the meeting, not after.** The purpose of the meeting is to make decisions. If an action item comes out of the meeting, it should have an owner and a deadline assigned before the meeting ends.

**No slides, no long reports.** The Scorecard is the agenda. Nothing else is presented unless it directly relates to a yellow/red metric. Reporting for the sake of reporting should happen asynchronously.

**90-day rule.** If a metric has been green for 12 consecutive weeks without exception, consider whether it belongs on the company scorecard or can move to a team scorecard. The company scorecard should include only metrics that require leadership attention.

## Connecting Scorecard to OKRs and Strategy

### The Scorecard-OKR Relationship

OKRs and the Scorecard serve complementary but distinct functions:

| Dimension | OKRs | Scorecard |
|---|---|---|
| Timeframe | Quarterly | Weekly |
| Purpose | Achieve stretch goals | Maintain operational health |
| Mindset | Ambitious / stretch | Threshold / alert |
| What it measures | Progress toward transformation | Current state of the business |
| Response when red | Adjust strategy or resource allocation | Immediate operational intervention |

OKRs tell you where you're going. The Scorecard tells you whether the engine is running well enough to get there.

A useful mental model: OKRs are the GPS destination. The Scorecard is the dashboard — fuel, temperature, speed. You need both to drive well.

### Cascading to Strategic Goals

The Company Scorecard should include at least one metric that tracks progress toward each of the major strategic goals in the Clarity Compass. If a strategic goal is "achieve $50M ARR in 3 years," the scorecard should track weekly ARR and monthly ARR pace vs. trajectory.

This prevents the common failure mode: strategic goals set in Q1 are forgotten by Q3 because day-to-day operational metrics crowd them out.

### Scorecard as OKR Early Warning System

When a scorecard metric turns red, the first question is: does this affect an OKR? If the Net Revenue Retention scorecard metric goes red, and there is an active OKR for "Drive NRR from 108% to 120%," that OKR should immediately be flagged as at-risk.

The Scorecard provides the weekly signal. The OKR review process provides the quarterly response.

## Building Your Scorecard: Common Pitfalls

### Too Many Metrics
More than 15 metrics means nothing is a priority. The discipline of a great scorecard is in what you leave out. If every department head demands their top 5 metrics on the company scorecard, you'll have 40 metrics and no company scorecard.

### Metrics No One Can Influence
If a metric appears on the scorecard but no one in the room has the authority or ability to change it, it doesn't belong on the scorecard. It belongs in a monitoring system.

### Vanity Metrics
Metrics that always look good (total users, cumulative revenue, total deals ever closed) provide false reassurance. The scorecard should be honest about where the business is now, not where it has been historically.

### Missing Early Warning Signals
A scorecard of only lagging indicators (revenue, churn, NPS) will tell you about problems that are 60–90 days old. Include leading indicators (new leads, pipeline created, trial starts, NPS verbatim flags) that surface problems before they become crises.

### Updating Frequency Inconsistency
A scorecard that is only updated by some owners, some of the time, destroys its own credibility. If the meeting starts and three metrics haven't been updated, the meeting should start with an acknowledgment of that — and a decision about whether to proceed. Over time, the team will update the scorecard before the meeting, or they'll stop attending a meeting that doesn't function.

## Scorecard Checklist

Before launching a company scorecard:
- [ ] 8–12 metrics selected (no more)
- [ ] Covers all three Value Engines (Growth, Fulfillment, Financial)
- [ ] Mix of leading and lagging indicators
- [ ] One named owner per metric
- [ ] Green / Amber / Red thresholds defined for each metric
- [ ] Weekly update process defined (who updates, by when)
- [ ] Weekly scorecard meeting scheduled at a fixed time
- [ ] North Star Metric identified and included
- [ ] Each metric's data source identified and accessible
- [ ] First 4-week dry run planned before treating any metric as authoritative
