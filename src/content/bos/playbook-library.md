# Playbook Library — Systematizing What Works

## What Is a Business Playbook

A Business Playbook is the documented system for how a key function or process in the business operates. It answers four questions: **Who** does **what**, **when**, and **how** — in enough detail that a competent new hire could execute it without asking a manager.

Ryan Deiss defines the Playbook Library in *Get Scalable* as the mechanism that converts founder-dependent or expert-dependent knowledge into organizational knowledge. The insight is simple but powerful: **if a process only works because the right person is doing it, you don't have a process — you have a person**. And people quit, get promoted, burn out, and go on vacation.

The Playbook Library is the difference between a business that scales and a business that adds complexity as it grows. Every $10M–$50M company that plateaus does so because it ran out of "key people" — individuals who could hold the operation together by sheer knowledge and effort. The Playbook Library removes this constraint by encoding key-person knowledge into the organization.

## When to Build Playbooks

### The Playbook Trigger

Not everything needs a playbook. The rule of thumb: **build a playbook for any process that needs to happen more than once, that affects customer outcomes or revenue, and that currently lives in someone's head.**

The three triggers for playbook creation:
1. **Repeatability**: The process happens regularly (weekly, monthly, quarterly, or with each new customer)
2. **Impact**: A mistake in this process costs money, customers, or employee time
3. **Dependency**: Execution currently depends on a specific person who may not always be available

### The Playbook Priority Matrix

Apply this matrix to identify which playbooks to build first:

| | High Repeatability | Low Repeatability |
|---|---|---|
| **High Impact** | Build immediately | Build before next occurrence |
| **Low Impact** | Build when resources allow | Optional |

Start with the top-left quadrant: processes that happen frequently AND have high impact. In most scaling businesses, these are:
- Customer onboarding
- Sales discovery call process
- Monthly financial close
- Performance review process
- New employee onboarding (hiring to day-30)

## Anatomy of a Business Playbook

A well-structured playbook has seven components:

### 1. Purpose and Scope
One paragraph explaining:
- What this playbook covers
- Why this process exists (what customer or business outcome it drives)
- What is out of scope (explicit boundaries reduce confusion)

### 2. Success Metrics
2–4 KPIs that define whether this process is working. These should be measurable and tracked on the team's weekly scorecard.

Example (Sales Discovery Playbook):
- Discovery-to-Demo conversion rate: target ≥ 60%
- Discovery call duration: 45–60 minutes (under 35 = insufficient qualification)
- Post-discovery opportunity score average: ≥ 7/10

### 3. Roles and Responsibilities (RACI)
Every playbook defines who does what. Use the RACI format:
- **R (Responsible)**: Does the work
- **A (Accountable)**: Owns the outcome, approves the result
- **C (Consulted)**: Provides input before action
- **I (Informed)**: Notified of outcome but not involved

Common mistake: multiple A's. Every task should have exactly one Accountable person.

### 4. Step-by-Step Process
The actual process, broken into numbered steps with enough detail to execute without interpretation. Each step includes:
- What to do
- How to do it (link to template, tool, or example if needed)
- What good looks like (quality standard)
- What to do if something goes wrong (escalation path)

### 5. Tools and Templates
Links to every tool, template, form, and software used in the process. The playbook is useless if it references a "proposal template" that doesn't exist or is three versions out of date.

### 6. Common Issues and How to Handle Them
A troubleshooting section documenting the top 3–5 things that go wrong and the standard response. This is the section that most often saves the business from recurring problems.

### 7. Review Schedule
When this playbook was last updated and when it should be reviewed again. Playbooks that are never reviewed become misleading quickly — processes change, tools change, team structures change.

## The Four Playbook Categories

### Category 1: Growth Playbooks

Growth Playbooks systematize how the business attracts, qualifies, and converts customers. Without them, sales and marketing quality depends entirely on who is in the role — which creates massive inconsistency as the team grows.

**Core Growth Playbooks every scaling business needs:**

**Inbound Lead Qualification Playbook**
- How to qualify an inbound lead within 24 hours
- Scoring criteria (what makes a lead worth pursuing)
- Response time standards (speed-to-lead benchmarks)
- CRM hygiene requirements (what to log, how to tag)

**Discovery Call Playbook**
- Pre-call research requirements
- Opening framework (how to set context and agenda)
- Core discovery questions (must-ask, not scripts — intent matters)
- Qualification criteria (what makes this worth pursuing to demo)
- Closing the discovery call (what happens next, clearly)

**Demo / Proposal Playbook**
- Demo structure (discovery insights → relevant use cases → outcome projection)
- Proposal template and pricing logic
- Objection handling guide (top 10 objections + effective responses)
- Follow-up cadence and timing

**Re-engagement Playbook**
- How to re-engage cold leads (when, how often, through which channels)
- What offer or message gets stale opportunities reactivated
- Cut-off point: when to disqualify rather than keep pursuing

### Category 2: Fulfillment Playbooks

Fulfillment Playbooks systematize how the business delivers on its promises. These are the highest-impact playbooks for customer satisfaction and NRR.

**Client Onboarding Playbook**
- Day 1: what happens the moment a new customer signs
- Week 1: technical setup, introductions, kickoff call
- Day 30: first review — is the customer on track for their success definition?
- Day 60: mid-point check — intervention if off-track
- Day 90: first renewal conversation if applicable

The onboarding playbook should specify:
- Who owns each step
- What the customer needs to do (and how you support them)
- What "good" looks like at each milestone
- Escalation criteria (if day-30 review reveals a problem, what happens?)

**Service Delivery Playbook**
- How core work is delivered (methodology, approach, communication cadence)
- Quality standards and review gates
- Communication templates (weekly update, monthly report, incident escalation)
- Change management: what happens when scope shifts

**Customer Success / Retention Playbook**
- Cadence of proactive outreach (not just reactive to problems)
- Health scoring: what signals indicate a customer is at-risk?
- Intervention playbook: what do you do for an at-risk customer?
- Renewal playbook: when to start renewal conversation, how to structure it

### Category 3: Operations Playbooks

Operations Playbooks systematize the internal processes that make the business run — finance, HR, IT, administrative functions.

**Financial Close Playbook**
- Monthly close checklist (who does what by what date)
- Reconciliation requirements and standards
- Financial reporting distribution (what goes to whom by when)
- Variance analysis: what triggers investigation?

**Hiring Playbook**
- How to open a requisition (requirements, approval, timeline)
- Job description template and standard sections
- Interview process (stages, who participates, evaluation criteria)
- Offer process (approval chain, offer letter template, negotiation boundaries)
- Background check and reference check process

**New Employee Onboarding Playbook**
- Pre-start: what's ready on day 1 (equipment, access, intro email)
- Week 1: company overview, team introductions, role clarity
- Month 1: training plan, first deliverables, check-in cadence
- Day 90: first formal performance review and 90-day retrospective

### Category 4: People Development Playbooks

People Development Playbooks define how the company grows its people — not just hires and fires.

**Performance Review Playbook**
- Review cadence (annual, semi-annual, or quarterly)
- Performance dimensions evaluated (outputs + values + development)
- Rating scale and calibration process (prevents grade inflation)
- Conversation framework (not just manager talking, employee contributing)
- Outcome documentation: what gets written down, where, and how it's used

**Promotion Playbook**
- What criteria trigger a promotion consideration
- Who makes the recommendation, who approves
- Compensation benchmarking process
- Announcement and transition management

**Performance Improvement Playbook (PIP)**
- Criteria for opening a PIP (specific behavior, not general dissatisfaction)
- PIP structure: timeline, expectations, check-ins, consequences
- Documentation requirements (legal protection, fairness)
- Outcomes: graduation criteria and exit criteria

## Building Your Playbook Library: The Sequencing

### Phase 1: Document Existing Excellence (Quarter 1)

Don't start by designing the perfect playbook — start by capturing what's already working. Interview the best performers in each function and ask:

1. "Walk me through exactly what you do from [start] to [end] of this process."
2. "What's the most common mistake new people make in this process?"
3. "What would you tell someone starting this role on Day 1?"

Document their answers. This is 70% of the playbook. The remaining 30% is quality standards, metrics, and escalation paths.

**Deliverables by end of Phase 1:**
- Inventory of all processes that need playbooks
- Priority matrix (high-impact, high-frequency first)
- 3–5 first playbooks drafted (the highest priority ones)

### Phase 2: Fill the Gaps (Quarter 2)

Review the playbooks from Phase 1 with a second person who wasn't involved in drafting them. Ask: "Could you execute this process using only this document?" The gaps they identify become Phase 2 work.

Also build the playbooks for processes that don't yet have an excellent performer — these processes need to be designed, not documented.

**Deliverables by end of Phase 2:**
- Tested and revised versions of Phase 1 playbooks
- 3–5 new playbooks (filling priority gaps)
- Playbook home established (where they live, how people find them)

### Phase 3: Systematize Reviews (Quarter 3 and ongoing)

Playbooks degrade. Assign each playbook an owner and a review date. Quarterly is usually right for high-frequency processes; annual is appropriate for stable, low-frequency processes.

Build the playbook review into the Meeting Rhythm: a quarterly agenda item is "which playbooks need updating this quarter?"

## Common Playbook Mistakes

### Too Long, Never Read

The biggest failure mode. A 40-page playbook will not be used. Target 2–5 pages for most processes. If it's longer, it's a manual — which is a different thing.

The solution: put the step-by-step process in the playbook and link to detailed appendices. The playbook is the map; the appendices are the territory.

### Not Maintained

A playbook that doesn't reflect current reality is worse than no playbook — it actively misleads. Build a review cadence and assign explicit ownership.

### Wrong Granularity

Some playbooks are too high-level ("Step 1: Research the customer") and some are too tactical ("Step 1: Open Chrome, navigate to LinkedIn"). The right level is: *what a capable new hire needs to know that they wouldn't learn from the tool or template itself.*

### No Metrics

A playbook without success metrics is a procedure, not a system. Every playbook must answer: how do we know this process is working?

## Playbook Library in the OrchestrA Platform

The Playbook Library connects to the BOS in two ways:

**1. Capability Mapping**: Each playbook represents a systemized organizational capability. When mapping capabilities, a playbook-backed capability is rated higher on the reliability and scalability dimensions than a person-dependent one.

**2. Scorecard Connection**: Each playbook's success metrics should appear on the owning team's weekly scorecard. The playbook is the process; the scorecard is the measurement system. Together they create a closed loop.

The BOS Agent uses playbook status as a signal of organizational maturity:
- No playbooks in a key area = high operational risk
- Playbooks exist but are outdated = moderate risk
- Playbooks are current, owned, and metrics are tracked = scalable operation
