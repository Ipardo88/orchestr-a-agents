# Meeting Rhythm — The Scalable Planning System

## Why Meeting Rhythm Is a Strategic Asset

Most companies treat meetings as a necessary evil — something to be minimized, optimized, or eliminated. Ryan Deiss's *Get Scalable* takes the opposite view: a well-designed meeting rhythm is one of the most powerful operating leverage points available to a scaling business.

The insight is this: **every decision, alignment moment, and course correction happens in a conversation**. The question is not whether those conversations happen — it's whether they happen at the right cadence, with the right people, with the right information, resulting in clear actions. Without a designed meeting rhythm, critical conversations happen randomly, too late, or not at all.

The **Scalable Planning System** — the BOS's meeting rhythm framework — creates five interlocking meeting cadences. Each cadence serves a distinct purpose, operates at a different timescale, and produces specific outputs that feed the next cadence. Together, they create a system where:
- Annual goals break into quarterly priorities
- Quarterly priorities generate weekly accountability
- Weekly accountability surfaces blockers
- Blockers get resolved before they become crises

## The Five Meeting Cadences

### Cadence 1: Annual Planning (Once Per Year)

**Purpose**: Set the direction for the year, align the leadership team, and connect the 3-Year Target to this year's priorities.

**Participants**: CEO + all direct reports (full leadership team)

**Format**: 2–3 day offsite, away from the office

**Typical Duration**: Full 2 days (not one day with an overnight — two full working days)

**Agenda Framework:**

**Day 1: Review and Reflect**
- Review the previous year's OKRs and scorecard results (2 hours)
  - What did we achieve? Where did we fall short?
  - What did we learn about the business? About ourselves as a leadership team?
- Review the 3-Year Target: Are we on track? Does the target still make sense? (1 hour)
- Review the Clarity Compass: Do our Values and Strategic Anchors still reflect who we are? (1 hour)
- External landscape review: What changed in the market, competitive environment, or regulatory context? (1 hour)
- Identify the 3 biggest challenges the business faces in the coming year (1 hour)

**Day 2: Plan and Commit**
- Define annual themes / priority areas (what the business needs most this year) (2 hours)
- Develop Company-level OKRs for the year (3 hours)
- Develop Q1 OKRs (1 hour)
- Cascade planning: each BU leader commits to their Q1 OKR direction (1 hour)
- Communication plan: how will we share the annual plan with the organization? (30 min)

**Outputs:**
- Updated 3-Year Target (if revised)
- Annual themes and priorities (2–4 themes)
- Company-level annual OKRs (3–5 objectives)
- Company-level Q1 OKRs (draft)
- Presentation / communication for the broader team

**Common Mistakes:**
- Running annual planning at the office (constant interruptions)
- Only planning the numbers (revenue goals) without planning the capabilities needed to achieve them
- Leaving without Q1 OKRs drafted (the planning document becomes a shelf artifact)
- Not involving BU leaders in the planning (they execute — they must be aligned, not informed after the fact)

### Cadence 2: Quarterly Sprint Planning (Every 90 Days)

**Purpose**: Translate annual priorities into 90-day operational commitments, assign owners, and set up the scorecard for the quarter.

**Participants**: CEO + direct reports, often with one level below for functional planning

**Format**: 1–2 day offsite or intensive workshop

**Why 90 Days?**

Ryan Deiss explicitly frames 90-day cycles as the optimal planning unit. Shorter periods (30 days) don't allow for meaningful strategic progress. Longer periods (6 months, annual) are too long to maintain accountability and adjust course. The 90-day sprint provides:
- Long enough to make real progress on meaningful goals
- Short enough to feel urgent and accountable
- Fast enough to surface and address strategy errors before they become expensive

**Agenda Framework:**

**Day 1: Review and Reset**
- Previous quarter OKR scoring (2 hours)
  - Score each KR on 0.0–1.0 scale
  - Qualitative retrospective: what did we learn?
  - What carry-forward and what closes?
- Scorecard review: 12-week trend analysis — what's the pattern in our health metrics? (1 hour)
- Identify Q3/Q4 implications: what did last quarter tell us about this quarter's priorities? (1 hour)

**Day 2: Set New Quarter Commitments**
- Company-level OKRs for the quarter (2 hours)
  - Maximum 5 objectives at company level
  - Each objective has 2–4 Key Results with baselines and targets
  - Owners assigned
- BU / functional OKRs (2 hours, parallel or sequential)
  - Each function sets OKRs that cascade from company OKRs
  - Cascade test: if every function achieves its OKRs, will the company OKRs be achieved?
- Scorecard targets for the quarter: for each scorecard metric, what is the target for the quarter? (1 hour)
- Resource allocation check: given these OKRs, are resources allocated correctly? Any reallocation needed? (30 min)

**Outputs:**
- Company and BU/functional OKRs for the quarter, published and visible
- Scorecard updated with quarterly targets
- Resource allocation changes (if any) decided and communicated

### Cadence 3: Monthly Business Review (Every Month)

**Purpose**: Mid-cycle health check — review scorecard progress, assess OKR trajectory, and make resource or priority adjustments.

**Participants**: CEO + direct reports

**Format**: Half-day in-office or virtual

**The Monthly Review is not a planning meeting** — it is a course-correction meeting. The plan was set in the Quarterly Sprint. The Monthly Review asks: are we on track, and if not, what do we do about it?

**Agenda Framework (Half Day):**

**Scorecard Review** (45 min)
- Review all company scorecard metrics: trends for the past 4 weeks
- Identify which metrics have moved materially (positive or negative)
- For each yellow or red metric: root cause discussion and proposed action

**OKR Trajectory Review** (45 min)
- For each company OKR: current progress vs. expected pace
- Flag any OKR that is at-risk of missing even 70% achievement by end of quarter
- Decision for at-risk OKRs: adjust scope, reallocate resources, or accept the partial achievement
- Decisions documented: "We decided to [action] because [rationale]"

**Key Decisions and Issues** (30 min)
- Any decisions that came to leadership between monthly reviews that need alignment
- Any emerging issues (competitive, market, people) that need proactive response

**Next Month Preview** (15 min)
- What's the most important thing to accomplish in the next 30 days?
- Any upcoming events (product launches, hiring completions, customer milestones) that need preparation?

**Outputs:**
- Documented decisions for any at-risk OKRs
- Updated scorecard targets if any were revised
- Clear priorities and owners for the next 30 days

### Cadence 4: Weekly Leadership Meeting (Every Week)

**Purpose**: Maintain operational alignment, review the scorecard, surface and resolve blockers, and make decisions that require leadership input.

**Participants**: CEO + direct reports (the same group as the Monthly Review)

**Format**: 60–90 minutes, fixed time each week

**This is the highest-frequency, highest-leverage meeting in the company.** Most leadership teams either don't have this meeting (and discover problems months late) or have it ineffectively (long, unfocused, no decisions made). A well-run Weekly Leadership Meeting is the single biggest operational leverage point available to the CEO.

**Standard 75-Minute Agenda:**

| Time | Segment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Wins (3 wins from the week) | Brief, positive, no discussion |
| 5–40 min | Scorecard Review | Every metric; owner speaks first |
| 40–65 min | Issues List | Problems that are yellow/red and need group resolution |
| 65–70 min | Action Items | Review last week's action items; assign new ones |
| 70–75 min | Cascade Communications | What needs to be communicated to teams this week? |

**Scorecard Review Protocol:**
1. Each metric is reviewed in order
2. Green metrics: owner says "Green, [number], no issues" — 15 seconds, move on
3. Yellow metrics: owner gives 60-second explanation and proposed response
4. Red metrics: owner gives root-cause analysis; group discusses solution; action item assigned

**Issues List Protocol:**
The issues list is a running list of problems, decisions, and discussions that need leadership input. Between weekly meetings, anyone on the leadership team can add to the issues list. In the meeting:
1. Identify the top 3–5 issues (if list is long, vote by show of hands or dot-voting)
2. For each issue: 10-minute discussion → decision or action item
3. Issues that cannot be resolved in 10 minutes get a dedicated session outside the weekly meeting (offload, don't expand the meeting)

**The "IDS" Pattern (Identify, Discuss, Solve):**
- **Identify**: What exactly is the issue? (Not "Sales is underperforming" — "Our win rate for enterprise deals has dropped from 32% to 18% over 6 weeks")
- **Discuss**: What are the root causes? (No more than 3–4 minutes)
- **Solve**: What is the action, who owns it, and by when? (1–2 minutes)

**Rules for an Effective Weekly Leadership Meeting:**
- Start on time, end on time — no exceptions. Late starts signal the meeting isn't important.
- Every metric must be updated before the meeting starts. If it's not updated, the owner explains why in the meeting.
- No slides. No prepared reports. The scorecard is the agenda.
- Every action item must have one owner and one deadline before the meeting ends.
- Meeting notes distributed within 24 hours.

### Cadence 5: Daily Stand-Up (Every Day, Team Level)

**Purpose**: Team-level coordination on daily priorities, surfaces blockers before they accumulate.

**Participants**: Individual teams (not the full leadership team)

**Format**: 15 minutes, standing, at a fixed time

**The daily stand-up is a team-level meeting, not a leadership meeting.** The CEO does not attend every team's stand-up. Individual team leads run this meeting for their team.

**Three Questions Format:**
Each team member answers three questions:
1. What did I complete yesterday?
2. What am I working on today?
3. Is anything blocking me?

**Rules:**
- 15 minutes maximum — if the meeting routinely goes over 15 minutes, something is wrong with the format
- Blockers are surfaced, not solved. If a blocker requires discussion, that discussion happens after the stand-up between the relevant people.
- The stand-up is a communication event, not a problem-solving session.

## Meeting Rhythm Cadence Summary

| Meeting | Frequency | Duration | Participants | Primary Output |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Annual Planning | Once per year | 2 days | CEO + all directs | Annual OKRs, 3-Year Target review |
| Quarterly Sprint Planning | Every 90 days | 1–2 days | CEO + all directs | Quarterly OKRs, scorecard targets |
| Monthly Business Review | Monthly | Half day | CEO + all directs | OKR trajectory decisions, next-30-day priorities |
| Weekly Leadership Meeting | Weekly | 75 min | CEO + all directs | Scorecard review, blockers resolved, actions assigned |
| Daily Stand-Up | Daily (weekdays) | 15 min | Individual teams | Daily coordination, blockers surfaced |

## Common Meeting Rhythm Failures

### The "We're Too Busy" Trap

The most common reason leadership teams cancel the weekly meeting, compress the quarterly sprint, or skip the monthly review is that everyone is "too busy." This is backwards: when the business is under pressure, the meeting rhythm becomes *more* important, not less. Canceling the structure that creates alignment during a crisis guarantees the crisis gets worse.

The solution: treat the meeting rhythm like a utility. You don't cancel electricity because work is busy. The meeting rhythm is infrastructure.

### All Reporting, No Deciding

Meetings that are purely informational — everyone presents their department, nothing changes — are productivity sinks. The Weekly Leadership Meeting is a decision-making forum. If a meeting ends and no decisions were made, either the wrong things were discussed or the format doesn't support decision-making.

Every meeting should end with at least 3–5 clear action items with owners and deadlines.

### Status Theater

"Status theater" is when the scorecard shows everything green — not because everything is fine, but because no one wants to bring a red metric to the group. This is the most dangerous meeting dysfunction because it erodes the meeting's core value: surfacing reality before it becomes a crisis.

Address status theater by rewarding the person who surfaces a problem early, and by the CEO modeling transparency about their own metrics first.

### Meeting Overload Without Integration

Many companies have too many meetings AND no meeting rhythm. They have recurring calls, check-ins, and syncs that overlap in purpose and participants, but no structured cadence that actually drives outcomes. The Scalable Planning System is not additive — it replaces the unstructured meeting overload with a designed system. On adoption, most companies can *reduce* total meeting hours while *increasing* meeting value.

## Implementing the Meeting Rhythm

### Phase 1: Launch the Weekly Leadership Meeting

Start here. Before building the full rhythm, establish the weekly cadence. The weekly meeting is the flywheel — once it's running, the quarterly and monthly cadences plug in naturally.

Week 1: Introduce the format. Don't try to be perfect — the goal is to establish the pattern.
Week 2–4: Refine the scorecard. What metrics need to be on it? Who owns what?
Week 5–8: Add the Issues List as a standing component. Begin tracking action items.

### Phase 2: Add the Monthly Review

Once the weekly meeting is stable (consistent attendance, consistent format, scorecard is being updated), add the monthly review. Use the existing scorecard as the foundation.

### Phase 3: Add the Quarterly Sprint Planning

Once monthly reviews are establishing a cadence of course correction, add the quarterly sprint. The first quarterly sprint is usually the hardest — OKRs may be poorly formed, cascade may be unclear. That's expected. The discipline improves with repetition.

### Phase 4: Annual Planning

By the time you reach the first annual planning session after implementing the Meeting Rhythm, the team has been through four quarterly sprints and twelve monthly reviews. The annual planning conversation is dramatically more grounded in data, more honest about challenges, and more specific about priorities than a planning process conducted without this foundation.
