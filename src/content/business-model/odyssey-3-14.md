# Odyssey 3.14 — Inventing and Reinventing Business Models

## What Is Odyssey 3.14

Odyssey 3.14 is a structured business model innovation methodology developed at HEC Paris after over 15 years of research, consulting, and teaching. The name encodes the method: **3 pillars** and **14 directions** for exploring business model innovation.

It complements the Business Model Canvas (BMC) by providing a directional thinking tool — while the BMC describes *what* a business model looks like, Odyssey 3.14 asks *where to look* for innovation opportunities. The two tools work together iteratively: Odyssey 3.14 identifies directions for change; the BMC maps out the resulting model.

Odyssey 3.14 applies to two contexts:
- **Reinventing an existing business model** — finding ways to evolve before a competitor or market shift forces you to
- **Creating a new business model** — designing from scratch around an underserved job-to-be-done

Business model innovation (BMI) is *not* a linear process. The methodology explicitly calls for iterating back and forth between the pillars and between the tools.

---

## The Three Pillars

Every business model is built on three interconnected pillars:

| Pillar | Question | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **Value Proposition** | What do we offer, and to whom? | Customer value, segments, revenue logic |
| **Value Architecture** | How do we deliver the value? | Resources, activities, value chain, partnerships |
| **Profit Equation** | What are the financial consequences? | Revenue, cost structure, capital employed, ROCE |

**Critical insight:** Value Proposition and Value Architecture drive the Profit Equation — they are the cause; profit is the effect. Innovation that only targets cost or revenue without changing the proposition or architecture is optimization, not business model innovation.

## Pillar 1: Value Proposition — 7 Directions for Innovation

The value proposition answers: *What value do we deliver to which customer, and why should they choose us?*

To innovate on the value proposition, explore these seven directions:

### Direction 1 — Reduce Clients' Overall Cost

Target the total cost of ownership, not just purchase price. Clients incur costs in three phases: **selecting** (search costs, comparison), **acquiring** (purchase price, delivery, installation), and **after-sale** (maintenance, support, switching costs).

Key questions:
- Can you strip down the offer to lower the price for the client?
- Can you offer a part of your product or service for free to increase volumes? (freemium model)
- Can you reduce peripheral costs the client incurs around your product?

**Paradigm examples:** IKEA (self-assembly eliminates delivery/installation cost), Ryanair (stripped service removes cost layers), Spotify (freemium converts non-consumers to payers)

### Direction 2 — Reduce Clients' Hassle

Identify the friction, complications, and workarounds clients encounter before, during, and after using your product. Hassle reduction often requires integrating adjacent products or simplifying complex processes.

Key questions:
- What products or services do clients use alongside yours?
- What constraints or complications do they encounter when using your offering?
- How can you remove or absorb these complications?

**Paradigm examples:** Netflix (eliminated video store hassle: browsing, late fees, availability), Tetra Pak (eliminated the need for refrigeration in packaging logistics)

### Direction 3 — Look for Non-Clients

Non-clients are potential customers who currently refuse, ignore, or cannot access your offer. Understanding *why* they don't buy reveals unmet needs and alternative market spaces. This direction connects to Blue Ocean Strategy's "demand creation" logic.

Key questions:
- What pushes potential clients to refuse or ignore your offer?
- Where do they go instead? What do they do?
- What elements of your offer disappoint or exclude them?

**Paradigm examples:** Hotel Formule 1 (designed for non-hotel-users: budget travelers who only needed sleep, not amenities), Metro (free daily newspaper for people who didn't buy papers)

### Direction 4 — More Functionality or Emotion

Every offer exists on a spectrum from purely functional to heavily emotional. Examine where yours sits and ask whether moving in either direction could create a new value proposition. Adding emotion creates brand premiums; stripping back to pure function can unlock price-sensitive segments.

Key questions:
- Is your product or service essentially functional or emotional in how clients experience it?
- How can you add a meaningful emotional dimension?
- Or conversely: how can you return to pure utility by removing emotional complexity?

**Paradigm examples:** Starbucks (coffee is functional; Starbucks added the "third place" emotional experience), Tesla (electric vehicle is functional; Tesla added status, performance, identity)

### Direction 5 — Search Other Segments or Industries

Existing market categories are defined by industry incumbents. The most powerful value propositions often come from importing rules or attributes from a completely different industry into your own. This is the source of most "category creation" companies.

Key questions:
- What are the codes, rules, and value attributes of other market segments you could integrate?
- What other business sectors could inspire your value proposition?
- What value attributes matter enormously in another industry but don't exist in yours?

**Paradigm examples:** Cirque du Soleil (imported theater production values into circus), Swatch (applied fashion rules to watch manufacturing to compete with cheap Asian alternatives)

### Direction 6 — Introduce a Third Party

Multi-sided business models create value by bringing together two or more distinct groups who benefit from interacting with each other. Identify whether there is a third party you could connect to your existing clients — or whether your assets could intermediate between two external groups.

Key questions:
- Could you identify a player that would benefit from access to your clients?
- For whom could you act as an intermediary?
- Could your assets (data, relationships, infrastructure) serve a third party?

**Paradigm examples:** Airbnb (connects homeowners with travelers — neither group existed in the other's market), Uber (connects drivers with riders, neither was previously a commercial participant)

### Direction 7 — Modify the Revenue Stream

Challenge how you charge, not just what you charge. Revenue model innovation often unlocks value that price changes cannot. Subscriptions, usage-based pricing, freemium, third-party subsidies, and reverse charging all represent fundamentally different revenue logic.

Key questions:
- Could you invoice clients differently (subscription vs. transaction vs. outcome-based)?
- What third parties would be interested in financing all or part of your offer?
- Does your offer allow clients to save money or generate income — could you take a share of that?

**Paradigm examples:** Google (advertisers subsidize free search for users), JCDecaux (cities receive free bus shelters; advertisers fund the model)

## Value Curve — The Blue Ocean Diagnostic

The **Value Curve** is a visual tool for mapping competitive positioning across the value attributes that matter to customers. It reveals where current offers converge (over-serving, over-investing) and where gaps exist (potential for new market space).

**How to construct a Value Curve:**

1. List all relevant value attributes — factors of competition as seen by customers (include attributes for any third parties)
2. For each attribute, assign a value score to each competing offer (your current offer, competitor offers, potential new offer)
3. Draw a curve for each offer connecting the attribute scores

**Reading the Value Curve:**

- Where curves converge: all players are competing on the same attributes, often over-investing in areas customers don't value distinctively
- Where your curve could be distinctive: lower on some attributes (reduce cost), higher on new attributes (create differentiation)
- A strongly differentiated new value curve that is radically different from all competitors = a Blue Ocean

**Connection to Odyssey 3.14 Directions 3, 4, and 5:** The Value Curve makes Direction 3 (non-clients) visible — non-clients are priced out or excluded by certain attributes. Directions 4 and 5 show where new value attributes could be added that competitors don't offer.

## Pillar 2: Value Architecture — 7 Directions for Innovation

The value architecture answers: *How does the organization create and deliver the value proposition?* It encompasses resources, competencies, activities, and partnerships.

### The Value Chain Foundation

The value chain maps the sequence of activities required to deliver the value proposition, from inputs through transformation to outputs. Michael Porter's model divides activities into:

**Primary activities:** Inbound logistics → Operations → Outbound logistics → Marketing & Sales → Service

**Support activities:** Firm infrastructure, Human Resource Management, Technology Development, Procurement

**Strategic resources and key competencies** are the root assets that make value chain activities possible. They include:
- **Tangible resources:** Financial capital, physical assets, manufacturing capacity
- **Intangible resources:** Patents, brand, data, algorithms, proprietary methods
- **Human resources:** Specific expertise, relationships, organizational knowledge

The distinction between *strategic* resources (critical for competitive advantage) and *generic* resources (necessary but not differentiating) is crucial. Nike outsourced shoe manufacturing (generic) while retaining product development and marketing (strategic).

### Direction 8 — Introduce Technology

Technology is often the source of value architecture transformation — either by enabling new activities or by replacing inefficient ones. Look beyond your own industry for technology that competitors in other sectors are using.

Key questions:
- Do competitors in other industries use technology you could integrate?
- What technologies in other sectors could transform how you deliver your value proposition?

**Paradigm examples:** Netflix (streaming technology eliminated physical distribution), Skype (VoIP technology eliminated telecom infrastructure dependency)

### Direction 9 — Modify Steps in the Value Chain

Rather than adding or removing activities, redesign how existing activities are performed. The goal is to find fundamentally more efficient or effective ways to execute the same step.

Key questions:
- What are the different activities in your value chain?
- Can you change or redesign the way you execute each activity?

**Paradigm examples:** Wikipedia (crowdsourced content creation replaced editorial production), Salesforce (cloud delivery replaced on-premise software installation)

### Direction 10 — Add or Remove Steps from the Value Chain

Activities that seemed necessary may be eliminable; activities not currently in your model may add value. Every step in the value chain should be justified by the value it adds to the proposition or the cost it prevents.

Key questions:
- Is each step in the value chain genuinely necessary? What happens if you eliminate it?
- Could you add a new step that would lower total cost or add customer value?

**Paradigm examples:** IKEA (eliminated assembly and delivery by having customers do it), Kilimo Salama (eliminated traditional claims assessors by using automated weather-data-triggered insurance payouts for African farmers)

### Direction 11 — Leverage Your Strategic Resources and Competencies

Strategic resources often have applications beyond the current business model. Under-exploited resources — data, brand, relationships, infrastructure, proprietary knowledge — can become the foundation of new revenue streams.

Key questions:
- What strategic resources do you under-exploit?
- Could you promote them in another way — to other customers or industries?
- Do you have know-how that could be valuable to other companies?

**Paradigm examples:** LinkedIn (leveraged professional network as a B2B recruitment database), Michelin Fleet Solutions (leveraged tire manufacturing expertise into fleet management and outcome-based pricing)

### Direction 12 — Associate with Competitors or Partners

Collaboration with competitors (coopetition) or with partners whose resources are complementary can create value that neither party could achieve alone. This requires clarity about which activities to compete on and which to collaborate on.

Key questions:
- What are competitors' strongest resources and capabilities?
- How are those strengths complementary to yours?
- Can you neutralize your weaknesses through association?

**Paradigm examples:** TED Conferences (partnered with independent organizers for TEDx events, multiplying reach without infrastructure cost), Google Earth (Google + NASA satellite data: neither could build the product alone)

### Direction 13 — Identify Supplementors

Supplementors are companies whose products or services are used by your clients in conjunction with your own offering — before, during, or after. Integrating supplementors creates a richer proposition and increases switching costs.

Key questions:
- What offers do your clients use before, during, and after your own?
- Which complementary offers would your clients most value if integrated?
- What would integration mean for the supplementor?

**Paradigm examples:** GoPro + Red Bull (GoPro cameras used by Red Bull athletes, Red Bull events filmed on GoPro — mutual amplification), Bilia + Volvia (car dealer + manufacturer-approved insurance combined at point of sale)

### Direction 14 — Find New Resources

The resource base of a business can be expanded beyond what is owned. Platforms, open innovation, community assets, and licensing create access to resources without ownership.

Key questions:
- How can you expand your base of tangible and intangible resources?
- Where can you look for resources far outside your current base?

**Paradigm examples:** Airbnb (leveraged homeowners' underutilized assets — rooms and homes — as the resource base for hotel-scale accommodation), LEGO Ideas (crowdsourced product design from fans, turning customers into co-developers)

## Pillar 3: The Profit Equation

The Profit Equation translates the Value Proposition and Value Architecture into financial consequences. The primary metric is **ROCE (Return on Capital Employed)**:

```
ROCE = Operating Result (OR) / Capital Employed (CE)
```

Which can be decomposed as:

```
ROCE = Operating Margin × Turnover of Capital Employed
     = (Revenue − Costs) / Revenue × Revenue / Capital Employed
```

**ROCE is increased by:**
- Increasing revenue (from VP innovations)
- Reducing costs (from VA innovations or revenue model changes)
- Reducing fixed assets (asset-light models, outsourcing)
- Reducing Working Capital Requirement (WCR): better receivables management, inventory reduction, extended payables
- Improving operating margin
- Increasing asset utilization

**The Profit Equation makes trade-offs explicit.** A business model that radically reduces price (Direction 1) must compensate by dramatically increasing volume, reducing WCR, or eliminating fixed asset investment. A subscription model (Direction 7) shifts revenue from lumpy transactions to recurring streams, improving capital planning and reducing revenue risk — but requires higher upfront customer acquisition investment.

**Working Capital Requirement (WCR):**
```
WCR = (Accounts Receivable + Inventory + Prepaid Expenses) − (Accounts Payable + Accruals)
```

Negative WCR (collecting from customers before paying suppliers) is a structural advantage: Amazon, Zara, and Netflix all benefit from this model.

## Using Odyssey 3.14 with Other Frameworks

Odyssey 3.14 is designed to work *alongside* other tools, not replace them:

| Tool | Purpose | When to Use Together |
|---|---|---|
| **Business Model Canvas** | Maps the current and target business model structure | Use BMC to document the model; use Odyssey 3.14 to find where to innovate |
| **Value Curve (Blue Ocean)** | Reveals competitive differentiation across value attributes | Use within Direction 3 (non-clients) and Direction 5 (other segments) |
| **Porter's Value Chain** | Maps internal activity sequence | Use as the foundation for Directions 9, 10, 11 |
| **Playing to Win** | Sets strategic choices for where and how to compete | Use Playing to Win to set the strategy; Odyssey 3.14 to innovate the model that executes it |
| **PESTEL** | Identifies macro-environment forces | Use PESTEL to identify external triggers that make certain Odyssey directions more urgent |

**The recommended sequence:**
1. Map the current business model on the BMC
2. Use PESTEL to identify environmental forces threatening or enabling the model
3. Use Odyssey 3.14 to explore 14 directions — generate innovation hypotheses for each
4. Plot new value propositions using the Value Curve
5. Evaluate innovation hypotheses against Playing to Win strategic choices
6. Model the Profit Equation impact of promising directions
7. Redesign the BMC to reflect the chosen innovation direction
8. Prototype and iterate

## Innovation Assessment Checklist

Before selecting an innovation direction, evaluate each hypothesis:

- [ ] Does this direction address a real unmet need (a job not currently being done well)?
- [ ] Does it change the value proposition in a way that is difficult to copy quickly?
- [ ] Does it alter the profit equation positively (higher margin, lower capital, better WCR)?
- [ ] Is it consistent with or does it reinforce the current Playing to Win choices?
- [ ] What must be true in the value architecture for this direction to work?
- [ ] Which existing resources or competencies can be leveraged?
- [ ] What new resources or partnerships would be required?
- [ ] What is the size of the opportunity (addressable market × revenue per customer)?
