# Business Model Canvas — Describing How Your Organization Creates, Delivers, and Captures Value

## Why the Canvas Before Strategy

Most strategy conversations start in the wrong place. Teams jump to competitive moves, new products, or market expansion before they can clearly describe how their current organization actually creates and captures value. The result: strategy debates where participants aren't talking about the same business.

Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur's *Business Model Generation* (2010) introduced the **Business Model Canvas** as a shared visual language for describing, analyzing, and innovating business models. It is not a strategy tool — it is a description tool. Before you can decide where to compete and how to win, you need an honest, precise description of your current model. The Canvas provides that.

The Canvas maps a business model onto a single page using nine interconnected building blocks. The left side (Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources, Cost Structure) represents the **efficiency engine** — what the organization does internally to create value. The right side (Customer Segments, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams) represents the **value engine** — how the organization reaches customers and captures value. The **Value Proposition** sits in the center, linking both sides: it defines what value is created (for whom) and what must be true on the left to deliver it.

The fundamental business model logic: **what you spend on the left must be more than recovered by the value you create on the right**. A Canvas with an impressive left side but an underpowered right side is a cost structure looking for a business.

## The Nine Building Blocks

### 1. Customer Segments (CS) — Who We Create Value For

The Customer Segments block defines the distinct groups of people or organizations the business serves. Choosing which segments to serve — and which to consciously ignore — is the first strategic decision embedded in the Canvas.

**Five segment archetypes:**

| Archetype | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Mass Market | No meaningful distinction between customers; one large group | Basic consumer goods |
| Niche Market | Serves a specific, specialized segment with specific requirements | Aerospace components supplier |
| Segmented | Similar customer groups with slightly different needs and problems | Retail bank serving mass affluent vs. high net worth |
| Diversified | Two or more unrelated segments with very different needs | Amazon: marketplace buyers and AWS cloud customers |
| Multi-Sided Platform | Two or more interdependent customer groups, each necessary for value | LinkedIn: job seekers and recruiters |

**When are two customer groups genuinely distinct segments?**

Treat them as separate segments when they differ on at least one of these criteria:
- Their needs require a meaningfully different Value Proposition
- They are reached through different Channels
- They require different types of Customer Relationships
- Their willingness to pay is substantially different
- Their profitability differs enough to warrant separate economics

**The discipline of choosing which segments NOT to serve** is as important as choosing which to serve. A Canvas that lists "all companies" or "everyone who needs X" in the CS block has not made a choice. It has avoided one.

**Key questions for CS:**
- Which specific customer segments are we creating value for today?
- Among those, which generate disproportionate value (revenue, margin, growth)?
- Which segments could we serve but have consciously decided not to — and why?
- Are there segments we are currently serving that we should exit?

### 2. Value Propositions (VP) — What Value We Deliver

The Value Proposition describes the bundle of products and services that create value for a specific Customer Segment. Each CS should have a distinct VP — or at minimum, VP should be mapped to which segments it serves.

**The job-to-be-done framing (Christensen):** Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a job. The job is functional (get me from A to B), emotional (make me feel confident), or social (signal something about who I am). Understanding the job reveals what the VP must solve. A great VP solves a real job better than current alternatives.

**Value types — what kind of value does the VP deliver?**

| Value Type | What It Means | Strategic Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Newness | Satisfies entirely new needs customers didn't previously perceive | Often technology-enabled; no reference price exists |
| Performance | Improving existing product/service performance | Subject to diminishing returns; customers stop valuing further improvement |
| Customization | Tailored to individual needs | Expensive to scale; powerful for premium segments |
| Getting the Job Done | Solving a complete problem end-to-end | High-value if market alternatives are fragmented |
| Design | Superior aesthetics or experience | Difficult to maintain; premium command depends on brand |
| Brand/Status | Value derived from being seen using it | Luxury, prestige, or professional signaling |
| Price | Lower price for equivalent value | Requires cost leadership; attracts price-sensitive segments |
| Cost Reduction | Helps customers lower their own costs | B2B dominant; VP must be quantifiable in ROI terms |
| Risk Reduction | Reduces risk customers take | Warranties, SLAs, guarantees, insurance-like models |
| Accessibility | Makes something available to customers previously excluded | Opens new markets; often enabled by technology |
| Convenience | Makes things easier to use or access | App vs. desktop; one-click vs. multi-step |

**VP specificity test:** A strong VP names the specific segment, names the specific job-to-be-done, and names the specific value type delivered. "We help [segment] accomplish [job] better than alternatives by delivering [value type]." A VP that reads "best-in-class solutions for businesses" fails this test entirely.

**Matching VP to CS:** In a multi-segment model, draw explicit links between each CS block and the VP it corresponds to. The VP for an enterprise customer (customization, risk reduction, SLA) is typically different from the VP for an SMB customer (price, convenience, self-service).

### 3. Channels (CH) — How We Reach Customers

Channels describe how the organization communicates with and reaches its Customer Segments to deliver a Value Proposition. Channels serve five distinct functions — a customer typically passes through all five:

| Phase | Function | Channel Question |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | How do we raise awareness of our products and services? | Marketing, content, paid acquisition, referrals |
| Evaluation | How do we help customers evaluate our Value Proposition? | Demos, trials, case studies, sales conversations |
| Purchase | How do we allow customers to buy? | E-commerce, sales rep, partner, in-store |
| Delivery | How do we deliver our VP to customers? | Onboarding, implementation, digital delivery |
| After-Sales | How do we provide post-purchase customer support? | Support desk, CSM, community, self-service |

**Channel types and trade-offs:**

| Channel Type | Reach | Margin | Control | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Direct Sales Force | High in targeted segments | High | High | High |
| Online/Digital | Very high | Very high | High | Low to medium |
| Partner/Reseller | High across geographies | Lower (share margin) | Lower | Lower upfront |
| Retail | High (consumer) | Low (retailer cut) | Low | Variable |
| Wholesale/Distributor | Wide | Low | Very low | Low |

**Key channel decisions:**
- Which channel phases are we best at? Where do customers fall off between phases?
- Are we using owned channels (direct), partner channels (resellers, platforms), or a mix?
- Do our channels match the buying behavior of our target segments? (An enterprise buyer doesn't want to purchase via e-commerce; a developer doesn't want a 6-week sales cycle.)
- Are our channel costs consistent with our margin model?

### 4. Customer Relationships (CR) — What Type of Relationship Each Segment Expects

Customer Relationships describe the types of relationships the organization establishes and maintains with each Customer Segment. They drive customer acquisition cost, retention rate, and expansion revenue potential.

**Six relationship archetypes:**

| Type | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Personal Assistance | Human interaction with a customer service rep | Complex, high-value, or early-stage customers |
| Dedicated Personal Assistance | One person assigned to one customer (Key Account Manager, CSM) | Enterprise accounts; high-touch, long-term relationships |
| Self-Service | No direct relationship; infrastructure provided for customers to help themselves | High-volume, low-margin, or technically capable segments |
| Automated Services | Personalized automated interaction (recommendations, alerts, auto-onboarding) | Digital products at scale |
| Communities | Connect customers with each other to solve problems | Products with high user knowledge value |
| Co-Creation | Customers participate in creating value (user-generated content, co-design) | Platforms; innovative B2B products |

**CR → Revenue Model connection:** The relationship type directly affects how you earn revenue.
- Personal assistance → project fees, retainers, advisory fees
- Dedicated personal assistance → enterprise subscription, managed service fees
- Self-service → product subscription, usage fees
- Communities → freemium, upsell, marketplace commissions

**CR diagnostic questions:**
- What relationship do our customers expect? What relationship do we actually deliver?
- Are our CR choices consistent with our VP? (Promising premium service but providing self-service is a fit failure.)
- What is our cost to acquire a customer (CAC) per segment? Is it sustainable given the revenue per customer?
- What is our retention and expansion model per segment? Is the CR architecture supporting it?

### 5. Revenue Streams (R$) — What Customers Are Willing to Pay For

Revenue Streams represent the cash a company generates from each Customer Segment. The critical question is not "what do we charge" but "for what value are customers willing to pay" — the distinction reveals where VP is strong and where it is weak.

**Revenue model archetypes:**

| Model | Description | Typical Margin | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Asset Sale | Transfer ownership of a physical or digital product | Variable | Hardware, software perpetual license |
| Usage Fee | Charge per unit of use | High (digital) | AWS, API calls, utility billing |
| Subscription Fee | Recurring fee for access | High | SaaS, media, managed services |
| Lending/Renting/Leasing | Temporary right to use an asset | Medium | Equipment leasing, car rental |
| Licensing | Permission to use IP | Very high | Patents, franchising, software licensing |
| Brokerage Fee | Commission for matching buyer and seller | Medium | Marketplaces, agencies, real estate |
| Advertising | Fee for access to an audience | Variable | Media, social platforms |

**Pricing mechanisms:**

| Mechanism | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed List Price | Published price for defined products | High volume, standardized offerings |
| Negotiated | Price determined through bargaining | Enterprise sales, complex deals |
| Dynamic/Yield Management | Price varies with time, demand, capacity | Hotels, airlines, SaaS usage tiers |
| Volume-Dependent | Lower unit price at higher volume | B2B discounts, wholesale |
| Auction/Reverse Auction | Market determines price | Commodities, procurement |

**Revenue Stream diagnostic questions:**
- How many distinct revenue streams do we have? Are they transaction-based or recurring?
- What proportion of revenue is recurring (predictable) vs. transactional (episodic)?
- Is there alignment between the value delivered (VP) and what we charge? Misalignment = revenue left on the table or unsustainable pricing.
- Which revenue streams are growing? Which are declining? What does that signal about the VP?

### 6. Key Resources (KR) — What Assets the VP Requires

Key Resources are the most important assets required to make the business model work — to deliver the VP, reach channels, maintain customer relationships, and earn revenue. Resources can be owned, leased, or acquired from Key Partners.

**Four resource categories:**

| Category | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Physical | Manufacturing facilities, equipment, distribution systems, machines | Factory, fleet, store network |
| Intellectual | Brands, patents, proprietary data, algorithms, domain expertise, customer data | Customer data, IP portfolio, methodology |
| Human | People with specialized skills, relationships, or domain knowledge | Sales talent, engineers, advisors |
| Financial | Cash reserves, credit facilities, equity, financial guarantees | Balance sheet, credit lines, investor relationships |

**Key Resource test:** A resource belongs in KR only if losing it would materially impair the VP or the business model. A company with a commodity IT system and generic office space has no physical Key Resources. A company whose Value Proposition is built on a proprietary algorithm has a critical intellectual Key Resource.

**Own vs. lease vs. partner:** Not all Key Resources need to be owned. The question is whether the resource is a source of competitive advantage (own it, protect it) or a commodity input (lease it, partner for it, don't invest in building it yourself).

### 7. Key Activities (KA) — What the Organization Must Do Well

Key Activities are the most important actions a company must perform to make its business model work. Like Key Resources, they are defined by the VP — what activities must be excellent for the VP to be delivered at the required level?

**Three activity archetypes:**

| Type | Description | Typical Business Models |
|---|---|---|
| Production | Design, manufacture, and deliver a product in substantial quantities or superior quality | Manufacturing, physical product companies |
| Problem-Solving | Developing new solutions to individual customer problems | Consulting, professional services, legal, medical |
| Platform/Network | Managing and developing a platform that enables others to transact or interact | Marketplaces, social networks, SaaS platforms |

**Activities vs. Resources — the common confusion:** Resources are assets (what you have); Activities are actions (what you do). A proprietary algorithm is a resource. Training and continuously improving that algorithm is an activity. Both can be Key — the confusion is writing an activity in the KR block or a resource in the KA block.

**KA test:** Would failing to perform this activity at a high level materially impair our VP delivery? If yes, it is a Key Activity. If it would cause inconvenience but not fundamentally damage the VP, it is a support activity — important but not strategic.

### 8. Key Partnerships (KP) — Who We Work With

Key Partnerships describe the network of suppliers and partners that make the business model work. No organization builds and owns everything it needs — partnerships allow companies to focus on what they do best while acquiring other capabilities through relationships.

**Four partnership types:**

| Type | Description | Primary Motivation |
|---|---|---|
| Strategic Alliance (non-competitors) | Partners with companies in adjacent spaces | Reach, capabilities, or resources neither has alone |
| Coopetition | Strategic alliance between competitors | Industry-wide standards, shared infrastructure, market development |
| Joint Venture | Formal shared entity to develop new business | New markets, high-risk opportunities, capital requirements |
| Buyer-Supplier | Optimized supply or distribution relationship | Cost efficiency, reliability, quality assurance |

**Three motivations for partnership:**
1. **Optimization and economy of scale**: Activities or resources that are more efficient when shared or outsourced (cloud infrastructure vs. building data centers)
2. **Risk and uncertainty reduction**: Sharing risk in uncertain ventures (R&D alliances, JVs in new markets)
3. **Acquisition of resources and activities**: Access capabilities you don't have and can't build quickly (channel partnerships, technology licensing)

**Partnership test:** Is this partner filling a gap in our Key Resources or Key Activities that would be costly or slow to fill internally? If not, it may be a vendor relationship rather than a Key Partnership — important operationally but not strategically defining.

### 9. Cost Structure (C$) — What Running This Model Costs

The Cost Structure describes all costs incurred to operate a business model. After defining the building blocks above, the costs should be derivable: costs flow from Key Resources, Key Activities, and Key Partnerships.

**Two business model archetypes by cost orientation:**

| Orientation | Priority | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Cost-Driven | Minimize costs wherever possible | Lean operations, automated services, outsourcing, price competition |
| Value-Driven | Focus on premium value creation; cost is secondary | High-touch service, premium materials, strong brand investment |

Most real models sit on a spectrum — neither purely cost-driven nor purely value-driven. The question is: where on the spectrum does your model need to be, given your VP and CS?

**Cost characteristics:**

| Characteristic | Description | Strategic Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed Costs | Remain constant regardless of volume (salaries, rent, infrastructure) | Require minimum revenue threshold; create operating leverage above threshold |
| Variable Costs | Scale with volume (COGS, usage-based cloud costs, commissions) | Lower breakeven risk; margin typically lower than fixed-cost models |
| Economies of Scale | Cost per unit falls as volume increases | Competitive advantage to large-scale operators |
| Economies of Scope | Cost advantages from operating in multiple areas simultaneously | Enables diversification with shared infrastructure |

## The Canvas as a Logical System

The nine blocks are not independent — they form a logical system where each block must support the others. The most important relationships:

- **CS → VP**: VP must solve a real job-to-be-done for each segment
- **VP → CH**: Channels must be capable of communicating and delivering the VP to the CS
- **VP → CR**: Relationship type must match the segment's expectations and the VP's promise
- **R$ → VP**: Revenue model must reflect the value customers actually receive
- **VP → KR + KA**: The left side must be configured to deliver what the right side promises
- **KR + KA + KP → C$**: Costs are the inevitable consequence of the operational choices on the left

**Filling the Canvas: recommended sequence**

1. Start with **CS** — who are we serving?
2. Move to **VP** — what value do we deliver to each segment?
3. **CH** — how do we reach them and deliver the VP?
4. **CR** — what relationship does each segment expect?
5. **R$** — how do we capture value from each segment?
6. **KR** — what assets are required to deliver the VP?
7. **KA** — what activities must we perform excellently?
8. **KP** — what do we access through partners?
9. **C$** — what does it cost to run this model?

## Common Canvas Errors

| Error | Description | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Vague CS | "Small businesses" or "enterprise customers" without meaningful segmentation | Name a specific segment: industry, size, geography, need |
| VP without a job | "Best-in-class software" with no reference to what customer problem is solved | Rewrite VP as "We help [segment] do [job] better than [alternative]" |
| Missing CH phases | Only listing "sales team" without considering awareness, evaluation, and after-sales | Map all five channel phases for each segment |
| Conflating KA and KR | Writing "our engineering team" in KR (it's a resource) and "building software" in KA (it's an activity) | Resources are nouns; Activities are verbs |
| Empty C$ | "Cost structure: operational costs" | List specific cost line categories derived from KR, KA, and KP |
| Missing revenue model logic | Listing revenue streams without connecting to VP or CS | Each revenue stream should be traceable to a specific VP for a specific CS |

## Canvas Examples

### B2B SaaS Company

| Block | Content |
|---|---|
| CS | Mid-market HR teams (100–1,000 employees); no dedicated HRIS team; growing headcount |
| VP | Automates employee onboarding end-to-end; reduces time-to-productivity by 40%; eliminates compliance errors |
| CH | Inbound content (SEO); PLG trial; inside sales for deals >$15K ARR |
| CR | Self-service onboarding for SMB; dedicated CSM for accounts >$30K ARR |
| R$ | Annual subscription ($5K–$80K ARR); implementation fees for enterprise |
| KR | Proprietary workflow engine; HRIS integration library; customer success playbooks |
| KA | Product development; customer onboarding; integration maintenance |
| KP | HRIS platform integrations (Workday, ADP); payroll partners |
| C$ | Engineering salaries (60% of OpEx); cloud hosting; customer success team |

### Professional Services Firm

| Block | Content |
|---|---|
| CS | Private equity-backed portfolio companies ($50M–$500M revenue) undergoing operational transformation |
| VP | Delivers measurable EBITDA improvement within 18-month engagement; removes execution risk from management team |
| CH | Partner referrals from PE firms; existing client relationships; conference presence |
| CR | Dedicated partner-led engagement; embedded on-site team; weekly steering committee |
| R$ | Monthly retainer ($80K–$200K/month); success fee tied to outcome metrics |
| KR | Senior partner expertise; proprietary diagnostic frameworks; implementation playbooks |
| KA | Client diagnostic; transformation design; execution program management |
| KP | PE firm relationships; specialist sub-consultants (IT, HR, supply chain) |
| C$ | Partner and senior consultant compensation (75% of revenue); travel and on-site delivery costs |

## Canvas and OrchestrA

The Business Model Canvas is Phase 4A of the OrchestrA strategy process. It produces the foundational description of how the organization currently creates, delivers, and captures value. This description is the input to:

- **BMC Assessment (4B)**: stress-testing the current model for fit, strength, risk, and innovation opportunity
- **PESTEL Analysis (4C)**: evaluating which external forces are reshaping the environment the model operates in
- **Playing to Win (4D)**: making deliberate strategic choices about which segments to serve, how to win, and what capabilities to build — choices that may require updating the Canvas

The Canvas is not a one-time document. Revisit it annually or whenever a major strategic shift is being considered. A business model that made sense in 2020 may need significant redesign by 2026.
