# Designing Business Models

This document explores the continuum between improving existing business models and inventing new ones. Understanding this spectrum is crucial for organizations aiming to balance the execution of known models with the exploration of innovative strategies. 

## Business Model Continuum

### Definition
The **Business Model Continuum** represents the spectrum between enhancing existing business models and developing new ones. On one end, companies focus on optimizing known models, while on the other, they experiment with unproven concepts.

### Key Questions
- How can a company effectively manage both existing and new business models?
- What strategies are necessary for incremental improvement versus radical innovation?
- How can organizations structure themselves to support both ends of the continuum?

### Examples
- **Existing Business Models**: A company like **Coca-Cola** continuously improves its distribution and marketing strategies to enhance its existing beverage offerings.
- **New Business Models**: **Amazon** has ventured into cloud computing with AWS, which was initially an unproven business model but has since become a significant revenue stream.

### How to Apply
1. **Assess Current Models**: Identify existing business models and evaluate their performance.
2. **Experimentation**: Allocate resources for small-scale experiments to test new business ideas.
3. **Adaptation**: Use insights from experiments to refine or pivot business models as necessary.
4. **Parallel Management**: Develop organizational structures that allow for simultaneous focus on both existing and new models.

## Execution vs. Exploration

### Definition
Execution refers to the process of optimizing and improving existing business models, while exploration involves the search for new, unproven business models.

### Key Questions
- What metrics should be used to evaluate success in execution versus exploration?
- How can organizations foster a culture that supports both execution and exploration?

### Examples
- **Execution**: **Procter & Gamble** focuses on improving product lines through market research and consumer feedback.
- **Exploration**: **Tesla** continually experiments with new technologies and business models, such as direct-to-consumer sales and energy solutions.

### How to Apply
1. **Set Clear Objectives**: Define what success looks like for both execution and exploration.
2. **Create Separate Teams**: Form dedicated teams for execution and exploration to maintain focus.
3. **Encourage Innovation**: Foster an organizational culture that values experimentation and learning from failure.

## Organizational Structures

### Definition
Different organizational structures are required to support the distinct needs of executing existing business models and exploring new ones.

### Key Questions
- What organizational changes are necessary to support both execution and exploration?
- How can leadership balance the priorities of both teams?

### Examples
- **Google** employs a dual structure, allowing teams to focus on core products while also dedicating resources to innovative projects like **Waymo** (self-driving cars).

### How to Apply
1. **Design Flexible Structures**: Create organizational frameworks that allow for agility and adaptability.
2. **Leadership Support**: Ensure leaders advocate for both execution and exploration initiatives.
3. **Resource Allocation**: Allocate budgets and resources based on the strategic importance of both existing and new business models.

## Key Takeaways
- The Business Model Continuum highlights the need for different strategies in improving existing models versus developing new ones.
- Execution focuses on optimizing known models, while exploration emphasizes experimentation and innovation.
- Organizations must create structures that support both execution and exploration to thrive in a competitive landscape.
- Successful companies balance the need for performance projections with the flexibility to adapt and pivot based on experimental outcomes.
- Leadership plays a crucial role in fostering a culture that values both incremental improvements and radical innovations.
