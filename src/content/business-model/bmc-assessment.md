# BMC Assessment — Stress-Testing Your Business Model

## Why Assess the Canvas Before Setting Strategy

A Business Model Canvas is a description. An assessment is an evaluation. Most organizations skip the assessment step and jump directly from describing their current model to designing strategy — a leap that produces strategy built on unexamined assumptions.

The BMC Assessment answers questions the Canvas itself cannot: Are the building blocks internally consistent? Which elements are genuinely strong versus merely habitual? Where is the model exposed to disruption, commoditization, or competitive erosion? Where is there untapped innovation potential?

Osterwalder's *Business Model Generation* (Strategy section, pp. 200–243) and Johnson, Christensen, and Kagermann's HBR article "Reinventing Your Business Model" (2008) provide the analytical foundation. The core insight from both: **most organizations don't know what their current business model actually is until they stress-test it**. The Canvas describes what you think you are. The Assessment reveals what you actually are and what strategic choices are implicit but unexamined.

The BMC Assessment is Phase 4B in the OrchestrA strategy process. It produces the strategic context for the choices made in Playing to Win (4D): it reveals your current "Where to Play" and "How to Win" choices — making them explicit and evaluable rather than implicit and assumed.

## The Four Assessment Lenses

Every building block should be evaluated through four lenses. Apply them sequentially — Fit first, then Strength, then Risk, then Innovation Opportunity.

### Lens 1: Fit — Does Each Block Support the Others?

Fit analysis asks whether the building blocks are logically consistent with each other. A business model with poor fit is internally contradictory — it promises one thing on the right side and delivers something different on the left side.

**Fit failures to look for:**

| Fit Failure Type | Example |
|---|---|
| VP-CS mismatch | VP claims "enterprise-grade security and compliance" but CS includes SMBs who can't afford or implement the required controls |
| VP-CR contradiction | VP promises "premium, high-touch service" but CR is self-service with no human contact |
| CH-CS mismatch | CS is senior executives at Fortune 500 companies but primary channel is cold outbound email |
| KR-VP gap | VP requires proprietary data advantage but KR section reveals the data is the same as competitors use |
| C$-R$ imbalance | Cost structure is dominated by high-touch delivery (professional services model) but Revenue Streams are subscription-based (product model) |
| KA-VP disconnect | VP is "fast, reliable delivery" but no KA addresses logistics, fulfillment, or quality assurance |

**Fit assessment process:** For each pair of adjacent blocks, ask: "If Block A is as we've described it, does Block B logically follow — or is there a contradiction?" Document every contradiction as a Fit issue.

### Lens 2: Strength — Which Elements Create Competitive Advantage?

Strength analysis identifies the elements of the business model that give the organization defensible competitive advantage. Not every element needs to be strong — the question is which elements are the moat.

**Strength signals:**

| Signal | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Hard-to-copy Resources | IP, proprietary data, exclusive relationships, brand that took years to build |
| Locked-in Customer Relationships | High switching costs, deeply embedded integrations, relationship depth that competitors can't replicate quickly |
| Network Effects | The product or platform becomes more valuable as more customers use it — makes replication increasingly difficult |
| Cost Advantage at Scale | Fixed cost investments that get amortized over a large customer base — competitors face cost disadvantage at smaller scale |
| Channel Exclusivity | Distribution relationships, partnerships, or access points that competitors cannot easily replicate |
| Unique Capability Stack | A combination of KA + KR + KP that no single competitor replicates (they may have one element but not all three) |

**Strength rating scale:** Rate each building block 1–5 on competitive strength:
- **5 (Very Strong)**: Defensible for 3+ years; competitors would take significant time and capital to replicate
- **4 (Strong)**: Defensible for 1–2 years; some replication risk
- **3 (Adequate)**: Table stakes; neither an advantage nor a disadvantage
- **2 (Weak)**: Below competitive standard; creates customer friction or cost inefficiency
- **1 (Critical Gap)**: Absent or severely impaired; threatens VP delivery

### Lens 3: Risk — Which Elements Are Exposed to Disruption?

Risk analysis identifies where the business model is vulnerable — to new entrants, technological change, customer defection, regulatory shift, or commoditization. Most organizations systematically underestimate model risk because they evaluate their business from the inside.

**Five key vulnerability signals (Osterwalder):**

**Signal 1 — Cost Structure Growing Faster than Revenue Streams**
If C$ is growing faster than R$ over multiple periods, the model is structurally deteriorating. This can happen even when revenue is growing. The trigger: growth is being funded by increasing costs rather than unlocked by operational leverage. Look for rising headcount per customer, increasing delivery cost, or escalating customer acquisition cost without a corresponding increase in LTV.

**Signal 2 — Customer Segments Shifting but VP Not Evolving**
CS demographics, needs, or buying behavior are changing (generational shift, industry consolidation, digital adoption) but the VP remains unchanged. The model is growing increasingly misaligned with the market it serves. Warning signs: declining win rates against new entrants, increasing price pressure, customers asking for things the model can't deliver.

**Signal 3 — Key Resources Becoming Commodities**
Resources that once differentiated the model are becoming available to anyone. Historical data advantage is eroded by public datasets. Proprietary software is replicated by open-source. Specialist human capital is distributed by the market. When KR commoditizes, the VP it supports weakens unless it is reinforced by new resources.

**Signal 4 — Channels Disintermediating**
Customers are finding ways to bypass the organization's channels — going direct to suppliers, using competitor channels, or self-serving what they previously needed assistance with. B2B: buyers doing research without engaging sales. B2C: customers purchasing via platform competitors. The warning sign: channel-sourced revenue declining even when total revenue is stable (other channels compensating).

**Signal 5 — Competitive Replication of KA and KR**
When competitors can replicate your Key Activities and Key Resources without extraordinary investment of time or capital, the How to Win logic is eroding. Test: could a well-funded new entrant replicate our core capability in 18 months? If yes, the model is not protected.

**Risk rating scale:** Rate each building block 1–5 on risk exposure:
- **5 (Critical Risk)**: Actively under attack; disruption impact in 0–18 months
- **4 (High Risk)**: Clear threat identified; likely impact in 1–3 years
- **3 (Moderate Risk)**: Trend visible but impact timing uncertain
- **2 (Low Risk)**: Possible but not probable in the strategic horizon
- **1 (Minimal Risk)**: No credible threat identified

### Lens 4: Innovation Opportunity — Where Could a New Approach Create Step-Change Value?

Innovation opportunity analysis asks: where in the Canvas could a fundamentally different approach create significantly more value — either for customers or for the organization? This lens shifts from diagnosis to possibility.

**Innovation opportunity types:**

| Opportunity Type | Question |
|---|---|
| CS Expansion | Are there adjacent segments we're not serving that our current KR and KA would enable us to serve? |
| VP Augmentation | Could we extend our VP to solve a broader or adjacent customer job — reducing customer effort to work with multiple providers? |
| Channel Innovation | Is there a channel model that delivers the VP more efficiently or reaches segments we currently can't reach affordably? |
| Revenue Model Shift | Could a different revenue model (subscription vs. transaction, usage vs. seat, outcome-based vs. time-based) better align with the value customers receive? |
| Resource Leverage | Do we have underutilized Key Resources (data, relationships, infrastructure) that could be deployed to create a new VP? |
| Ecosystem Play | Could we shift from a bilateral model to a platform model — enabling other parties to transact or create value within our ecosystem? |

## Cross-Block Dependencies Matrix

Not all blocks are equally coupled. Some changes cascade through the entire model; others are more contained. Mapping dependencies before assessing the model reveals which changes are high-leverage and which are isolated.

| Block Changed | Primary Impact On | Secondary Impact On |
|---|---|---|
| CS (new segment added) | VP (may need new proposition), CH (new channels to reach), CR (different relationship type) | R$ (new pricing), KR (new capabilities), C$ (new cost to serve) |
| VP (VP expanded or changed) | KA (new activities required), KR (new resources needed), C$ (new costs) | CH (different delivery), R$ (different price point) |
| Revenue Model (R$) | C$ (must align with margin), CR (support model must match price) | KA (activities that deliver to the model), KP (partners sharing revenue) |
| Key Resource (KR) acquired | KA (activities change), KP (may replace a partner), C$ (new cost category) | VP (may enable new proposition), CS (may enable new segment) |
| Channel (CH) disrupted | CS (segment accessibility), CR (relationship model changes) | R$ (channel economics change), KP (partner channel affected) |

**How to use the matrix:** Before deciding to change any building block, identify all primary and secondary impacts. A change to Revenue Streams that seems small often requires changes across 4–5 other blocks to maintain internal consistency.

## The Johnson/Christensen Business Model Change Triggers

Johnson, Christensen, and Kagermann (HBR, 2008) identified five circumstances that signal a new business model — not just a refinement of the current one — may be required:

**Trigger 1: New Customer Segment Locked Out by Price or Complexity**
There is a large group of potential customers who cannot use the current offering because it is too expensive or too complex. If a simpler, cheaper alternative could serve them, and your current model cannot profitably deliver it, a new model is needed. The opportunity is not to serve these customers with a lower-margin version of the current model — it is to redesign the model from scratch around their actual job-to-be-done.

**Trigger 2: New Technology With No Viable Business Model**
A technology exists (internal or external) that could solve a real customer problem but cannot be deployed profitably within the current business model. This often occurs when a transformative technology requires a different cost structure, a different go-to-market, or a different customer relationship than the existing model supports.

**Trigger 3: Job-to-Be-Done Not Addressed by Any Current Model**
A customer job is being left unsatisfied — customers are using workarounds, cobbling together multiple partial solutions, or simply going without. No current competitor has a model that solves the complete job. The opportunity is to design a model around solving the whole job rather than competing on features within the current model definition.

**Trigger 4: Low-End Disruptors Threatening the Core**
A competitor is growing in the low end of the market with a simpler, cheaper offering. Current customers are not defecting yet — the disruptor's offer is clearly inferior on the traditional performance dimensions. But the disruptor is building scale, improving quality, and ascending into the core market. Ignoring this (because the core margin is still healthy) is the classic innovator's dilemma. A new, separate business model may be required to compete.

**Trigger 5: Shifting Competitive Basis**
The basis of competition is moving — from product features to service, from service to ecosystem, from ecosystem to data. What customers value most is changing, and the current model is optimized for yesterday's basis of competition. Organizations that don't redesign the model remain excellent at things that matter decreasingly.

## Scoring Framework

Apply the scoring framework to produce a structured, defensible assessment output.

**Block-Level Scoring:**

| Building Block | Strength (1–5) | Risk (1–5) | Overall RAG | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Customer Segments | | | | |
| Value Propositions | | | | |
| Channels | | | | |
| Customer Relationships | | | | |
| Revenue Streams | | | | |
| Key Resources | | | | |
| Key Activities | | | | |
| Key Partnerships | | | | |
| Cost Structure | | | | |

**RAG rating logic:**
- **Green**: Strength ≥ 4 AND Risk ≤ 2 — current block is an asset; protect and extend
- **Amber**: Strength 3–4 AND/OR Risk 3 — requires attention; specific improvement action needed
- **Red**: Strength ≤ 2 OR Risk ≥ 4 — requires urgent action; current state threatens VP delivery or financial sustainability

## BMC Assessment Output

A completed BMC Assessment produces three deliverables:

**Deliverable 1: Block-by-Block Summary Table**
The scoring table above with one sentence of narrative per block explaining the key finding. Total length: 1 page.

**Deliverable 2: Top 3 Strategic Implications**
The three most important conclusions from the assessment — not observations, but implications for strategic choices. Example format:
- "Our VP for [segment X] is not differentiated — three competitors offer equivalent functionality at lower price. Without a new source of differentiation, margin in this segment will compress to zero within 24 months."
- "Our channel model is efficient for reaching existing buyers but structurally unable to reach [new segment Y] — capturing that segment requires a channel investment or a partnership model."
- "Our cost structure is 30% above the industry average for our revenue scale. This gap is structural (high-touch delivery model) and cannot be bridged without a VP or CR redesign."

**Deliverable 3: Model Change Assessment**
Rate the current model on each of the five Johnson/Christensen triggers. If two or more triggers are active, a new business model design — not strategic refinement — is warranted.

## Common BMC Assessment Findings

| Finding Type | What It Looks Like | Strategic Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Over-reliance on one CS | One segment = >60% of revenue; no secondary segment with meaningful scale | Revenue concentration risk; strategic imperative to diversify or deepen defensibility in core |
| VP too broad | VP description covers 3+ value types for 2+ segments with no clear priority | Lack of focus typically means below-average execution on all VPs; choose where to be exceptional |
| Revenue model misaligned | VP delivers sustained value (ongoing benefit) but R$ is transactional (one-time sale) | Value captured is a fraction of value delivered; recurring model conversion is the strategic move |
| KR commoditizing | Resources listed as KR are available to all competitors and improving at market | The moat is eroding; new proprietary resources must be built |
| CR-cost mismatch | High-touch CR (dedicated CSMs) applied to low-revenue segments | Cost to serve exceeds margin; segment-level economics are negative |
| Channel dependence | 70%+ of customer acquisition through a single channel (e.g., one partner, one platform) | Channel concentration risk; platform or partner holds pricing and reach power |

## Connection to Playing to Win

The BMC Assessment directly informs the Playing to Win strategic choices (Phase 4D):

- **Where to Play**: The assessment reveals which CS blocks are genuinely attractive (strong VP fit, growing segment, defensible relationship) and which are liabilities (thin margins, high churn, weak competitive position). WTP choices should double down on the former and exit or deprioritize the latter.
- **How to Win**: The Strength analysis reveals where the current model creates advantage. The Risk analysis reveals where that advantage is eroding. HTW choices should build on real strengths and address the most critical risks before they become existential.
- **Must-Have Capabilities**: The KA and KR blocks, assessed for strength and gap, directly inform which capabilities need to be world-class for the chosen HTW to work.

The assessment is complete when the team can answer: "If we stay on our current trajectory with our current model, what is the most likely outcome in 3 years?" Honest answers to this question — enabled by the assessment — are the foundation for genuinely strategic choices.
