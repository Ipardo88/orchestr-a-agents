## Capital Allocation Fundamentals

Capital allocation is the most important responsibility of the CEO. It describes how a company raises and spends money and plays a central role in value creation. The appropriate objective: **maximize long-term value per share**.

Two imperatives drive good capital allocation:
1. **Competition**: Poor capital management leads to marketplace losses against better-managed rivals
2. **Opportunity cost**: All capital has an opportunity cost; capital that fails to earn the cost of capital destroys value

The "valuation quotient" (sum of valuation-dependent decisions / market cap) averaged 14% from 1985-2021, showing how much capital allocation matters for shareholder returns.

Key challenge: CEOs rarely have capital allocation training. The skills that enable someone to become CEO are often different from those required for effective capital allocation. Warren Buffett: "Once they become CEOs, they face new responsibilities. They now must make capital allocation decisions, a critical job that they may have never tackled and that is not easily mastered."

## Sources and Uses of Capital

**Sources**: Internal financing (operating cash flow, asset sales) is the primary source. External sources include equity issuance and debt issuance. Companies growing faster than their ROIC need external capital.

**Uses** (ranked by typical magnitude):
1. **M&A**: Largest redistribution mechanism (~13.5% of sales in peak years, highly cyclical)
2. **Investment SG&A ex-R&D**: Intangible investment (~7.1% of sales, growing as economy shifts to intangible-intensive)
3. **Capital Expenditures**: Physical investment (~5.7% of sales, declining share)
4. **Gross Buybacks**: Shareholder returns (~5.4% of sales, growing)
5. **Dividends**: Shareholder returns (~3.4% of sales, stable)
6. **Investment R&D**: Innovation investment (~2.3% of sales, growing)
7. **Net Working Capital**: Operating capital (~0.2% of sales, declining in importance)
8. **Divestitures**: Source of cash (~2.8% of sales on average)

Major structural shift: From tangible to intangible investment. Tangible investment was 2× intangible in the 1970s; that ratio has flipped. This reflects the rise of IT and healthcare sectors (from ~20% of S&P 500 in 1985 to >40% in 2021).

## Capital Allocation Alternatives

### M&A
- By far the largest source of capital redistribution
- 9 in 10 public companies did ≥1 deal in the 1990s-2000s
- Key equation: NPV of deal = PV of synergies − premium
- Average premium: ~45% (equal-weighted), ~30% (size-weighted)
- Cost synergies achieved: 36% of companies exceed targets; revenue synergies: only 17%
- Cash deals outperform stock deals
- Operational deals outperform transformational deals
- Companies with specialized M&A teams outperform
- Success rate improving: 36% → 40% → 44% across recent decades
- EPS accretion has NO empirical link to value creation (most populated box in research: positive EPS + negative abnormal return)

### Capital Expenditures
- ~35% is investment (above depreciation); ~65% is maintenance
- Announcements of capex increases generally well-received by market
- Benefits follow inverted U-shape: too little and too much both destroy value
- Track maintenance vs. growth capex separately for meaningful analysis

### R&D
- ~75% should be considered investment, ~25% maintenance
- R&D productivity (RQ) declining in recent decades
- ~⅓ of companies spend below optimal, ~⅔ spend too much, ~5% near optimal
- R&D efficiency (cost to launch) differs from R&D effectiveness (value per launch)
- Large pharma has effectiveness; small companies have efficiency → explains M&A pattern

### Divestitures
- Generally create value for shareholders (meta-analysis of ~100 studies confirms)
- Sellers typically capture >100% of deal value in M&A
- Spin-offs create value for both parent and spun-off entity
- Academic papers on M&A outnumber divestiture papers 6:1, creating an attention bias
- Executives are reluctant to divest (admitting past mistakes, preference for growth)
- Small percentage of assets often creates most of the value → many businesses fail to earn cost of capital

### Dividends
- Remarkably resilient across economic cycles
- ~35% of US companies pay dividends (down from ~75% in late 1970s)
- CFOs view dividends as commitments equal to investment decisions
- Dividend stability is ~4× that of buybacks
- Dividends signal confidence in future earnings sustainability
- Most investors do NOT reinvest dividends back into the paying stock

### Share Buybacks
- More volatile than dividends; viewed as discretionary
- The "golden rule": Repurchase only when stock trades below expected value AND no better investment opportunities exist
- Buying undervalued stock transfers wealth from sellers to continuing holders
- Buying overvalued stock transfers wealth from continuing holders to sellers
- Paying dividends treats all shareholders equally regardless of stock price
- ~⅓+ of recent buybacks offset dilution from stock-based compensation
- 76% of CFOs say increasing EPS is important motivation (but NO empirical link to value creation)
- On average, companies can time equity transactions to benefit ongoing shareholders

### Working Capital
- Net working capital has declined from ~30% to <10% of assets over recent decades
- Cash conversion cycle (CCC) varies dramatically: Energy median 33 days, Healthcare 127 days
- Negative CCC possible (Amazon: -38 days) when payables exceed current assets
- Companies managing CCC well deliver higher shareholder returns
- Excessive cash holdings: top 10 companies hold 25% of total; top 80 hold 50%

## Assessing Capital Allocation Skills

### Past Spending Patterns
Analyze incremental rates (investment per $1 change in sales):
- Incremental M&A rate
- Incremental intangible investment rate
- Incremental fixed capital rate
- Incremental working capital rate

Example: Microsoft (5yr ending FY2022): M&A 33.9%, intangibles 32.2%, fixed capital 47.4%, working capital -8.6%.
Example: Cisco: M&A 144.2%, intangibles 21.7%, fixed capital -44.8%, working capital -16.0%.

Look for inflection points (Azure driving Microsoft capex increase; Mondelez freeing $4.5bn by taking CCC from +39 to -37 days).

### ROIC and ROIIC
- **ROIC** = NOPAT / Invested Capital (aim to exceed cost of capital)
- Average adjusted ROIC for Russell 3000: 9.1% (1990-2021)
- ~70% of observations showed ROIC exceeding 5% cost of capital threshold
- **ROIIC** = ΔNOPAT / ΔInvested Capital (measures change on the margin)
- Calculate over 3-5 year rolling periods to reduce noise
- Rising ROIIC signals improving capital efficiency OR positive operating leverage

### Corporate Governance and Incentives
- Look for a "North Star of value"—executives grounded in long-term value per share
- Assess governing objective (shareholder vs. stakeholder orientation)
- Evaluate agency costs: managers may favor growth over shareholder returns
- Compensation analysis: 69% use TSR as long-term incentive metric (good in principle but can be distorted)
- SBC as % of sales: ~9% for small companies, ~1% for large; ~13.4% median for healthcare, 0.6% for materials
- ~80% of financial executives would pass on a value-creating investment to make near-term earnings
- CFOs are systematically overconfident (overprecision) in their forecasts
- Decision-making is sticky: status quo bias affects capital allocation year over year

### Life Cycle Considerations
- Introduction: High investment, negative returns
- Growth: Rising investment, improving ROIC
- Mature: Peak ROIC, shift toward shareholder returns
- Shake-out: Declining returns, restructuring needs
- Decline: Capital return exceeds investment

## Five Principles of Capital Allocation

### 1. Zero-Based Capital Allocation
The question: "What is the right amount of capital to have in this business to support the strategy that creates the most wealth?" Based on the future, not inertia.

Critical finding: 98-99% of companies are below the optimal reallocation threshold. More proactive reallocation → higher ROA. Companies good at internal allocation are also effective at external allocation.

### 2. Fund Strategies, Not Projects
Capital allocation should support strategic goals, not just pass rate-of-return tests on individual projects. A good project within a bad strategy should not be funded. A marginal project within a winning strategy may deserve funding. What matters is the value of the bundle.

Watch for the institutional imperative: "Any business craving of the leader, however foolish, will be quickly supported by detailed rate-of-return and strategic studies prepared by his troops." (Buffett)

### 3. No Capital Rationing, But Earn Sufficient Returns
Replace "capital is scarce but free" mindset with "capital is accessible but comes at a cost." Capital can come from: redeploying from underperforming divisions, selling businesses, accessing capital markets. All capital has an opportunity cost, whether from internal or external sources.

### 4. Zero Tolerance for Bad Growth
Companies should invest in innovation while cutting losses when a strategy is unlikely to pay off. Recognize the value of quitting. Divestitures reduce cross-subsidization risk and free the best employees for value-creating businesses. Amazon has a long list of failed initiatives—the key is killing them early.

### 5. Know the Value of Assets and Be Ready to Act
Great capital allocators always know the difference between price and value in all businesses. They are willing to act when gaps become large. Executives usually prefer buying to selling, even though sellers generally do better. Overcome inertia to build long-term value.

## Capital Allocation Checklist

**Past Spending Patterns:**
- □ Analyze historical capital allocation, separating operating uses from payouts
- □ Determine financing sources
- □ Assess management's framework for thinking about capital uses
- □ Examine whether spending mix has changed over time
- □ Consider whether new management intends different allocation
- □ Map who makes which capital allocation decision

**ROIC and ROIIC:**
- □ Calculate level and trend of ROIC
- □ Compare ROIC vs. peers; identify differences and improvement sources
- □ Compute ROIIC over multiple periods; identify marginal changes

**Governance and Incentives:**
- □ Look for evidence of a "North Star of value"
- □ Determine whether company has articulated governing objective
- □ Assess potential agency costs
- □ Evaluate executive compensation metrics and magnitude
- □ Ask whether compensation practices influence capital allocation
- □ Judge appropriateness of time horizon

**Five Principles:**
- □ Is capital allocation zero-based? Does the company overcome inertia?
- □ Is the company investing to support strategies (not just projects)?
- □ What is the attitude about cost of capital and access to capital?
- □ Is the company willing to exit value-destroying businesses?
- □ Does management know the difference between price and value? Ready to act?

## Internal Capital Markets

### Capital Balance Concept (McKinsey)
Companies must balance investment opportunities against capital supply:
- **Capital-short companies** (more opportunities than capital): Aggressive on divestments, higher hurdle rates, consider raising capital
- **Capital-long companies** (more capital than opportunities): Accept lower return thresholds, reluctant to divest, return cash to shareholders

Three types of capital decisions:
1. **Reinvestment in existing businesses**: Maintaining competitive position, product development
2. **Large new investments**: Acquisitions, new market entries, dramatic business shifts
3. **Capital from exits**: Selling businesses, especially those with scarce assets commanding premiums

### Natural Ownership and Investment Returns
- Calculate net return (5-10 year horizon) from all portfolio moves: keep, invest in step-changes, acquire, sell
- Always calculate returns relative to current value (not book value)
- The ideal investment: natural ownership leads to superior net returns
- The ideal portfolio: enough such investments to deploy all available capital above cost of capital
- Accounting returns mislead: book value ≠ market value, creating bias toward keeping lackluster businesses

## Portfolio-Level Resource Allocation

### Role-Based Allocation
Translate portfolio roles into differentiated resource allocation:

| Role | Capital | Talent | Management Attention |
|------|---------|--------|---------------------|
| Growth Engine | Priority access, above-average | Best talent, recruiting priority | High strategic involvement |
| Value Creator | Adequate for optimization | Strong operational leaders | Regular performance review |
| Cash Generator | Maintenance only | Efficiency-focused leaders | Light touch, financial KPIs |
| Development | Milestone-gated funding | Entrepreneurial talent | Active coaching and monitoring |
| Exit Candidate | Minimal, preserve sale value | Transition planning | Exit preparation focus |

### KPI Design
- Use ROIC as foundation across portfolio (more accurate than profit alone)
- Complement with role-specific KPIs: growth businesses need user/subscriber metrics; mature businesses need efficiency metrics
- Set KPIs based on understanding true value drivers of each business AND what "good" means (external benchmark)
- Financial steering example: Stellantis uses net cash flow as guiding metric across holistic efficiency program

### Operational Steering Models
Spectrum from "Active Operator" to "Strategic Holding":
- **Active Operator** (Samsung, Unilever model): Deep involvement in technology, M&A, strategy of each portfolio business. Best for similar businesses where synergies drive significant value.
- **Strategic Holding** (TATA, A.P. Møller model): Coordinating role while each business retains significant autonomy. Best for diverse portfolios benefiting from autonomy.

Trade-off: Excessive synergy focus → slow decision-making. Excessive freedom → lost scale benefits.
