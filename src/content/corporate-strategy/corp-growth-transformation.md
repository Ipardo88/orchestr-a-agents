## Importance of Corporate Growth

Growth is the most important long-term value-creation lever of corporate strategy. The systematic search for growth options complements portfolio analysis. Growth strategy may involve searching for new growth areas beyond the existing portfolio and should ultimately be reflected in the target portfolio.

Key growth challenge: Most companies' core businesses mature over time, requiring proactive search for next-generation growth engines before current engines stall.

Only ~25% of growth initiatives succeed. Three-quarters of the top business disasters of the past five years involved growth initiatives gone wrong. Yet sustained high-growth companies achieve 3× average growth rates.

### BCG Growth Framework
Distinguishes growth sources:
- **Momentum growth**: Riding market tailwinds
- **M&A growth**: Acquiring additional revenue
- **Market share gains**: Taking share from competitors
- **Adjacent growth**: Expanding into related areas

### Adjacency Map (Zook/Bain)
Visualize expansion options radiating outward from the core:
- New geographies
- New customer segments
- New channels
- New products/services
- New value chain steps
- New businesses using core capabilities

## Growth Within the Existing Portfolio

### Innovation-Driven Growth
- **Core innovations** (~70% of investment): Incremental improvements to existing products for existing customers
- **Adjacent innovations** (~20%): Enter new markets or serve new customers with extended offerings
- **Transformational innovations** (~10%): New products for new customers, serving previously unmet needs

The "Golden Ratio" (70/20/10) outperforms—companies following it typically realize a P/E premium of 10-20%. Returns are roughly the inverse: core generates ~10% of innovation returns, adjacent ~20%, transformational ~70%.

### Organic Growth Barriers
Common barriers preventing companies from exploiting growth potential:
- Internal budgeting processes that favor existing businesses
- Risk aversion in mature organizations
- Lack of entrepreneurial talent and culture
- Insufficient investment in growth capabilities
- Short-term financial pressure crowding out long-term growth investments
- Organizational complexity slowing decision-making

### Market Expansion
- Geographic expansion (domestic → regional → global)
- Customer segment expansion (adjacent segments with similar needs)
- Channel expansion (direct → indirect, physical → digital)
- Use-case expansion (new applications for existing capabilities)

## Growth Beyond the Existing Portfolio

### Adjacency Moves (Zook Framework)

Adjacency moves are expansions into areas away from, but related to, the core business. They are the primary vehicle for sustained profitable growth.

**Adjacency Types**:
1. **Product adjacencies**: New products sold through existing channels to existing customers
2. **Geographic adjacencies**: Existing products in new geographies
3. **Value chain adjacencies**: Forward or backward integration
4. **Channel adjacencies**: New distribution channels for existing products
5. **Customer adjacencies**: New customer segments with related needs
6. **New-to-the-company business adjacencies**: Using core capabilities in entirely new domains

**Repeatable Formula**: The most successful growth companies develop a "repeatable formula"—a standardized adjacency pattern they can execute quickly and repeatedly. This creates a learning curve advantage.

**Evaluation Criteria for Adjacency Moves**:
1. Strength of the core business (prerequisite—don't expand from weakness)
2. Relatedness to the core (more related = higher probability of success)
3. Profit pool attractiveness of the target adjacency
4. Feasibility given existing capabilities and resources
5. Ability to achieve leadership position in the new space
6. Alignment with broader corporate strategy and vision

**Success rates decline with distance from core**: One-step adjacencies succeed more often than multi-step ones. The further from core capabilities and knowledge, the higher the risk.

### Unrelated Diversification
- Historically poor track record for most companies
- Works primarily when: (a) the company has superior corporate management capabilities, (b) capital markets are inefficient (emerging markets), (c) the company can transfer specific operational excellence across businesses
- In underperforming industries, diversification is an advantage; in overperforming industries, focus pays off

### Acquisition Target Search
- Define strategic criteria based on portfolio gaps and growth ambitions
- Screen universe systematically (industry, geography, size, ownership)
- Assess strategic fit AND ownership advantage
- Pre-build relationships with priority targets
- Maintain a prioritized pipeline of 10-20 targets per growth theme

## M&A as a Growth Vehicle

### Value Creation in M&A
The fundamental equation: **NPV of deal = PV of synergies − premium paid**

If the present value of achievable synergies exceeds the premium, the deal creates value for the buyer. If not, wealth transfers from buyer to seller.

### Key Research Findings
- Average deal premium: ~45% (equal-weighted) or ~30% (size-weighted)
- Buyer's stock goes down 60% of the time upon announcement (average: -1.6%)
- Initial market reaction is informative and relatively unbiased—poor results are modestly more likely to persist than positive ones
- Success rate has improved over time: 36% positive (1995-2002) → 40% (2003-2010) → 44% (2011-2018)

### What Works in M&A
- **Cash deals** outperform stock deals (signals management confidence)
- **Operational/bolt-on deals** outperform transformational deals
- **Companies with specialized M&A teams** outperform (>⅓ of public companies have them)
- **Lower premiums** correlate with better outcomes
- **Programmatic acquirers** (≥2 deals/year) outperform selective and large-deal acquirers
- **Early movers** in M&A cycles do better than late movers

### What Destroys Value
- Hubris: bidding more than justified by synergies
- EPS accretion focus: no empirical link between EPS change and value creation
- Revenue synergy optimism: only 17% of companies achieve revenue synergy targets vs. 36% for cost synergies
- Transformational deals: radically new strategic direction carries highest risk
- Institutional imperative: imitating competitor M&A without independent analysis

### Synergy Assessment
- Cost synergies are ~2× more reliable than revenue synergies
- Market deems synergies more credible when management shares concrete figures with clear explanation
- Common pitfalls: underestimating one-time integration costs, unrealistic market share assumptions, customer loss underestimation
- Quantify synergies rigorously: compare PV of after-tax synergies / cost of capital vs. premium

### SVAR (Shareholder Value at Risk)
- Cash deal SVAR = premium / buyer market cap
- Stock deal SVAR = premium / (buyer market cap + seller market cap including premium)
- High SVAR deals deserve substantial scrutiny
- SVAR measures the wealth transfer if no synergies materialize

## Strategic Alliances

When full acquisition isn't optimal, alliances provide intermediate options:
- Joint ventures (shared equity, shared control)
- Strategic partnerships (contractual, capability sharing)
- Minority investments (financial exposure with strategic insight)
- Licensing agreements (technology or brand access)

Alliances work best when: objectives are aligned, capabilities are complementary, governance is clear, exit provisions are defined, and the partnership creates value neither partner could achieve alone.

## Divestitures

### Why Divest
- Business is worth more to another owner (better owner test)
- Business doesn't fit the target portfolio strategy
- Capital can be redeployed to higher-returning opportunities
- Shedding allows parent to focus on remaining businesses
- Addition by subtraction: value of proceeds exceeds value of operation as part of portfolio

### Research Findings
- Divestitures generally create value for shareholders (meta-analysis of ~100 studies)
- Sellers tend to do better than buyers in M&A transactions
- Spin-offs create value for both the spun-off entity and the parent
- Drivers of spin-off value: sharpened managerial focus, better information, improved governance

### Executive Reluctance
- Divestiture often means admitting a past mistake
- Managers prefer growth over shrinkage (more responsibility and compensation)
- Internal political resistance
- Remedy: charge independent people (board, CEO, CFO) with final portfolio decisions

### Divestiture Types
- **Outright sale**: Sell to strategic buyer or PE firm
- **Spin-off**: Tax-free distribution of subsidiary shares to existing shareholders
- **Equity carve-out**: IPO of subsidiary while retaining partial ownership
- **Partial stake sale**: Sell minority stake to financial investors (free up capital, maintain control)

### Timing Triggers
- Natural inflection point in asset lifecycle
- Looming need for significant capital investment
- Favorable market conditions (high valuations in target industry)
- Loss of ownership advantage (better owner emerged)
- Strategic redirection of parent company

## Large-Scale Portfolio Transformation

Some companies undergo transformational portfolio changes, essentially rebuilding the portfolio around a different strategic core.

**Examples**: dsm-firmenich (mining → nutrition), Apple (desktop → mobile), SoftBank (telco → software).

**Key Success Factors**:
1. Clear strategic vision for the target portfolio
2. Sufficient financial resources and flexibility
3. Strong execution capabilities (deal-making + integration)
4. Patience—transformation typically takes 5-10 years
5. Stakeholder management (investors, employees, regulators)
6. Willingness to make decisive exits from legacy businesses

## Portfolio Refresh Rate

Research on the world's largest companies (McKinsey, 2007-2017):

| Refresh Category | Refresh Rate | Excess TSR | Companies |
|-----------------|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Ponds** (static) | <10% | -0.5% | 53% |
| **Rivers** (balanced) | 10-30% | **+5.2%** | 23% |
| **Rapids** (hyperactive) | >30% | +1.5% | 23% |

"Rivers" that also use **programmatic M&A** (≥2 deals/year): **+6.2%** excess TSR.

Companies that start with negative portfolio momentum but move into higher-momentum industries achieve +1.7% excess TSR. Companies that stay in declining industries without corrective action: negative excess TSR.

**Refresh rate** = change in revenue mix of the company over 10 years, measured by sum of absolute changes in share of revenues of all business units.

**Implication**: Active but disciplined portfolio rotation (not too little, not too much) is the strongest driver of long-term TSR. Inactivity is the most common mistake.
