# An Introduction to Corporate Strategy

This document provides an overview of **corporate strategy**, its key components, and the framework that assists CEOs in strategic decision-making. Understanding corporate strategy is crucial for aligning an organization’s vision with its operational capabilities and market opportunities.

## What is Corporate Strategy?

**Corporate strategy** refers to the overarching strategy that guides an organization’s entire portfolio of business units. It focuses on industry trends, resource allocation, and the organization’s vision and mission. This contrasts with **business unit strategy**, which targets specific competitive advantages within individual business units.

### Key Differences Between Corporate Strategy and Business Unit Strategy

| Aspect                     | Corporate Strategy                                      | Business Unit Strategy                               |
|---------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| Focus                      | Entire business unit portfolio                          | Immediate business environment                        |
| Responsibility             | Vision, mission, investor relations                     | Competitive advantage through new models, products  |
| Reporting Structure        | Reports to CEO                                         | Operates independently within corporate framework    |

## Corporate Strategy Framework

The corporate strategy framework consists of six core themes, each addressing critical questions for CEOs and boards during a corporate strategy review:

1. **Corporate Vision and Objectives**
2. **Corporate Portfolio Strategy**
3. **Corporate Growth Strategy**
4. **Parenting Strategy**
5. **Capital Allocation Strategy**
6. **Investor Strategy**

### 1. Corporate Vision and Objectives

**Definition**: The corporate vision outlines where the organization aims to be in the next five to ten years.

**Key Questions**:
- What is the organization's identity and values?
- How will the organization achieve its vision?

**Examples**: A company like Microsoft may revise its vision to focus on cloud computing and AI, reflecting industry shifts.

**How to Apply**:
- **Step 1**: Collect propositions for vision themes.
- **Step 2**: Synthesize options for vision statements.
- **Step 3**: Crystallize the vision through board selection and cultural testing.
- **Step 4**: Communicate the vision organization-wide.

### 2. Corporate Portfolio Strategy

**Definition**: This strategy defines the organization’s business unit portfolio logic and roles.

**Key Questions**:
- Which business units are performing well?
- What actions are needed for underperforming units?

**Examples**: General Electric has historically used portfolio management to assess and divest from underperforming units.

**How to Apply**:
- Use matrix-based analysis to categorize business units based on market attractiveness and competitive strength.
- Identify units for investment or divestment based on their growth potential.

### 3. Corporate Growth Strategy

**Definition**: This strategy focuses on identifying incremental or transformative growth opportunities.

**Key Questions**:
- Where will growth come from?
- Are current innovation levels sufficient?

**Examples**: Amazon’s expansion into grocery with the acquisition of Whole Foods illustrates a growth strategy through diversification.

**How to Apply**:
- Map growth options using the "where to play" and "how to win" framework.
- Explore various growth levers such as geographic expansion and innovation.

### 4. Parenting Strategy

**Definition**: This strategy aligns the corporate center's role with the organization’s operating model.

**Key Questions**:
- How can the corporate center create value?
- What support do business units need?

**Examples**: Procter & Gamble leverages its corporate center to provide branding and marketing support across its product lines.

**How to Apply**:
- Assess the perceived value of the corporate center through interviews and surveys.
- Identify gaps and areas for improvement in support functions.

### 5. Capital Allocation Strategy

**Definition**: This strategy defines how financial and human resources are allocated across the organization.

**Key Questions**:
- How is capital currently allocated?
- Should investments be shifted based on performance?

**Examples**: Apple’s strategic investments in R&D and acquisitions reflect a calculated approach to capital allocation.

**How to Apply**:
- Create a transparent portfolio and funding process.
- Regularly evaluate and prioritize investments based on strategic alignment.

### 6. Investor Strategy

**Definition**: This strategy focuses on attracting the right investors and articulating a compelling investment thesis.

**Key Questions**:
- What do investors value?
- How can the organization communicate its investment thesis effectively?

**Examples**: Tesla’s focus on sustainability and innovation attracts investors aligned with its vision.

**How to Apply**:
- Identify the organization’s natural investor type.
- Develop communication materials that highlight the investment thesis.

## Key Takeaways

- **Corporate strategy** encompasses the entire organization, focusing on long-term vision and resource allocation.
- The **corporate strategy framework** includes six themes that guide strategic decision-making.
- Each theme raises critical questions that CEOs must address to ensure organizational alignment and performance.
- Engaging in a **corporate strategy review** can be triggered by external trends, performance issues, or leadership changes.
- A balanced approach combining **creative thinking** and **analytical processes** is essential for effective strategy development.
