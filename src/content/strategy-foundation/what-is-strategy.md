# What Is Strategy? It's a Lot Simpler Than You Think

This document explores the concept of **strategy** as articulated by Felix Oberholzer-Gee, emphasizing its simplicity and foundational elements. Understanding strategy is crucial for organizations aiming to create and capture value effectively.

## Definition of Strategy

**Strategy** is a plan to create value. It encompasses how a company intends to generate value for its customers, employees, and suppliers. While financial metrics like profitability and return on invested capital are often seen as indicators of strategy, they are outcomes rather than starting points. 

### Key Questions
- What does it mean to create value?
- How can a company increase its value creation?
- What are the components of a successful strategy?

### Examples
- **Best Buy**: Faced with competition from Amazon, Best Buy transformed its strategy to enhance customer experience and employee engagement, leading to significant profitability improvements.

### How to Apply
- Focus on the future and how to create value rather than just analyzing past financial performance.

## Understanding Value Creation

Value is defined as the difference between **willingness to pay** and **willingness to sell**. 

### Willingness to Pay
- Represents the maximum price a customer is willing to pay for a product or service.
- Example: A customer may be willing to pay $7 for a cup of coffee but buys it for $2 at Dunkin' Donuts, creating significant value.

### Willingness to Sell
- Refers to the minimum compensation an employee would accept to work for a company.
- Example: An employee may consider job offers from multiple companies and choose based on job satisfaction and compensation.

### Total Value Creation
The total value created by a company is the difference between willingness to pay and willingness to sell, which is then divided among:
- Customers (difference between willingness to pay and price)
- Employees (difference between willingness to sell and compensation)
- The company (profit margin)

## Increasing Willingness to Pay

There are three primary ways to increase willingness to pay:

1. **Quality of Product or Service**
   - Higher quality leads to increased customer appeal and willingness to pay.

2. **Complements**
   - Products or services that enhance the value of another product can increase willingness to pay.
   - Examples: Razor and razor blades, printers and cartridges.

3. **Network Effects**
   - The value of a product increases as more people use it.
   - Example: Social media platforms like Instagram become more valuable as more friends join.

## Decreasing Willingness to Sell

To attract talent, companies can either:
1. **Increase Compensation**
   - Higher salaries can make a company more competitive in the talent market but do not create new value.

2. **Enhance Job Quality**
   - Improving working conditions, training opportunities, and flexibility can lower willingness to sell, creating actual value.

### Example: Best Buy's Strategy Shift
- Best Buy faced significant challenges but turned around by:
  - Treating stores as warehouses to improve shipping times, increasing willingness to pay.
  - Collaborating with brands to create "stores within stores," enhancing the shopping experience and lowering costs.

## The Value Stick Framework

The **Value Stick** is a visual representation of how value is created and distributed among customers, employees, and the company. 

| Component                | Description                                                                 |
|--------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Willingness to Pay      | Maximum price customers are willing to pay for a product or service.       |
| Willingness to Sell      | Minimum compensation employees would accept to work for the company.       |
| Value Created            | Difference between willingness to pay and willingness to sell.              |
| Distribution of Value    | Split among customers, employees, and the company’s profit margin.         |

## Key Takeaways
- Strategy is fundamentally about creating value, not just analyzing financial outcomes.
- Value creation is defined by the difference between willingness to pay and willingness to sell.
- Companies can increase value by improving product quality, leveraging complements, and utilizing network effects.
- Attracting talent can be achieved through higher compensation or by enhancing job quality.
- The Value Stick framework helps visualize how value is created and distributed within an organization.
