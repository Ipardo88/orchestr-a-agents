# Vision Design — The Concrete Picture of Where You Are Going

## Why Vision Is the Most Misused Word in Strategy

Ask ten executives to show you their company vision, and you will get ten different things: a paragraph of aspirational language, a tagline, a list of values, a description of what the company does, or a copy of the mission statement with "great" added. Almost none of them will be a vision in the strategic sense of the word.

A vision, properly defined, is a specific, concrete, time-bounded picture of the future state the company will achieve. It names real numbers, real markets, real customer outcomes, and a real deadline. It is close enough to feel achievable and far enough to require genuine transformation. It is specific enough that in the target year, you can run a simple test: did we achieve it, or did we not?

This is Phase 3B of the OrchestrA Strategy Foundation. It builds directly on the purpose statement captured in Phase 3A and produces a company-level vision that will anchor the strategic goals work in Phase 3C.

---

## Vision Defined: The Three Requirements

A statement qualifies as a vision only if it meets all three of the following:

**1. Specific and Concrete**
A vision must name a measurable outcome, not describe a quality. "Become the most trusted brand in healthcare" is not specific — "most trusted" is not measurable. "Serve 10 million patients with accessible, same-day mental health care by 2030" is specific — you can count patients, you can measure same-day access, and you know when 2030 arrives.

**2. Time-Bounded**
A vision without a deadline is a wish. The deadline creates the urgency that forces resource allocation decisions. It also creates the feedback mechanism: at the deadline, did you get there? If not, what must change?

**3. Ambitious but Credible**
A vision must stretch the organization — it should require capabilities the company doesn't currently have, resource levels it doesn't currently deploy, and operational maturity it hasn't yet achieved. But it must stop short of fantasy. A vision no one believes is achievable produces cynicism and learned helplessness. A vision everyone can see the path to produces activity, not transformation.

---

## The Paradox at the Heart of Vision

There is a genuine tension in vision design that cannot be resolved — only managed:

**Ambition vs. Specificity.** A highly ambitious vision tends toward abstraction ("change the way people think about money"). A highly specific vision tends toward modesty ("grow revenue 30% by 2027"). The goal is a vision that is simultaneously ambitious in scope and specific in content — names the number, names the market, names the change in the world, and names the deadline.

**Inspiration vs. Constraint.** A vision must inspire — people need to feel something when they hear it, a pull toward a future worth working for. But it must also constrain — it should make some activities obviously right and others obviously wrong. A vision that doesn't rule anything out isn't a vision; it's permission to do anything.

**Permanence vs. Adaptability.** A vision should not change every time the quarterly forecast changes. But a company that holds to a vision that has become clearly unachievable wastes years of energy on a dead end. The rule: revisit annually, revise rarely, replace only when the business model or market context has changed fundamentally.

## Jim Collins' BHAG: Four Types of Ambitious Vision

In *Built to Last*, Jim Collins and Jerry Porras introduced the concept of the **Big Hairy Audacious Goal (BHAG)** — a long-horizon (10–25 year) vision so ambitious that it requires the organization to become something it is not today. A BHAG is not the same as a 3-year target; it operates at the aspirational level of the purpose, translated into a concrete destination.

Collins identified four types of BHAGs:

### Target BHAG
A specific, quantified target to be achieved. The most common type. Clear, measurable, and unambiguous.

*Examples:*
- "Become a $125B company" (Walmart, stated in 1990 when it was a $1B company)
- "Put a man on the moon and return him safely to earth before this decade is out" (NASA, 1961)
- "Reach $1 trillion in market cap" (various tech companies)

**Best for:** Companies in growth phases where scale is the primary strategic question.

### Compass BHAG
A directional vision that doesn't specify a single quantified target but clearly points in a defined direction. Often more appropriate when the market or product is still evolving.

*Examples:*
- "Democratize the automobile" (Ford, early 20th century) — not a specific unit count, but a clear direction
- "Make the most of the Internet era" — too vague, a bad compass BHAG
- "Give every person on earth a phone-based financial account by 2035" — specific direction, ambitious timeframe

**Best for:** Companies where the strategic choice is directional (which market, which customer) more than quantified (how many units).

### Role Model BHAG
"Become the [X] of our industry" — using a recognized external benchmark as the definition of the destination.

*Examples:*
- "Become the Harvard of the West" (Stanford, in its early years)
- "Become the Nike of the cycling industry"
- "Become what Salesforce is to CRM, for [vertical]"

**Best for:** Companies in emerging categories where they need to calibrate ambition against a known external standard. Risk: the role model becomes outdated or the comparison stops serving as motivation.

### Internal Transformation BHAG
Not about market position or scale, but about becoming a fundamentally different organization. Often appropriate for established companies facing existential change.

*Examples:*
- "Transform from a hardware company into a services company" (IBM's shift in the 1990s)
- "Become the world's most efficient operator of [X]" (operational transformation focus)
- "Create a culture where 70% of revenue comes from products that didn't exist five years ago" (3M-style innovation aspiration)

**Best for:** Established companies that need fundamental organizational transformation, not just growth.

## Time Horizons: How Vision Stacks Across Timeframes

A well-designed vision architecture has three layers, each operating at a different time horizon and serving a different strategic function.

| Horizon | Label | Time Frame | Primary Function | Appropriate Ambition Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Operational** | 3-Year Target | 3 years | Define the near-term destination; anchor annual planning; make goals concrete | Achievable with extraordinary effort |
| **Strategic** | 10-Year Vision | 10 years | Define the company's strategic aspiration; anchor multi-year investment decisions | Requires capabilities not yet built |
| **Aspirational** | BHAG | 15–25 years | Capture the ultimate mission-driven destination; anchor culture and purpose | May require luck, breakthroughs, or market shifts |

Most early-to-mid-stage companies need only the 3-Year Target and the 10-Year Vision. The BHAG becomes useful when the organization is large enough and mature enough that a 10-year vision feels near-term.

**How the layers relate:**
The BHAG is the mountaintop. The 10-year vision is a specific campsite on the way up the mountain. The 3-year target is the next ridge you need to reach. The annual strategic goals are this season's ascent plan.

A company that can articulate all three layers has a coherent strategic narrative. A company that has only a BHAG and no 3-year target has inspiration without traction. A company that has only a 3-year target and no BHAG has a financial plan, not a strategic vision.

## Ryan Deiss's 3-Year Target: Making Vision Operational

Ryan Deiss, in *Get Scalable*, introduces the **3-Year Target** as the primary vision tool for growing companies (roughly $1M–$100M revenue range). The 3-Year Target is explicitly not about aspirations — it is about painting a vivid, specific, achievable picture of where the company will be in exactly three years.

The 3-Year Target answers four questions with specific, named answers:

**1. Revenue: What is our annual revenue in Year 3?**
Not a range. Not "approximately." A specific number. "$18.5M" not "$15–20M." The specificity forces commitment and creates a clear test.

**2. Customers/Volume: How many of what kind of customer are we serving?**
Name the segment, name the count. "2,200 mid-market SaaS companies with 50–500 employees" is a 3-Year Target answer. "More enterprise customers" is not.

**3. Market Position: What is our position in the market?**
This is the qualitative descriptor of how you are known. "The recognized authority on revenue operations for mid-market B2B SaaS" or "the default payroll solution for construction companies with 25–250 employees."

**4. Operational Maturity: What does the business look like internally?**
How is it running? What has been systematized? What can operate without the founder in the room? "A company that can operate with the CEO out of the building for 60 days, with documented playbooks for every revenue function and a leadership team that runs weekly execution reviews independently."

**The Deiss Test for a Valid 3-Year Target:**
Read it aloud to your leadership team. If the response is "that's exciting and a little scary," it is calibrated correctly. If the response is "that's unrealistic," scale it back. If the response is "we could probably do that in 18 months," make it more ambitious.

## What Belongs in a Vision Statement

A complete vision for a company at Phase 3B should include:

**Metric or State:** A specific, measurable outcome — revenue, customer count, market share, geographic reach, product capability, operational metric. The more specific the better.

**Time Horizon:** A named year. "By 2028" not "within the next few years."

**Market/Customer Specificity:** Which market, which customer segment. "Small business owners" is not specific. "Independent restaurant owners in the United States with 1–5 locations" is specific.

**The "So That" Clause:** What changes in the world, or for customers, as a result of achieving this vision. This connects vision back to purpose. "So that first-generation entrepreneurs can access the same quality of financial planning that enterprise companies take for granted."

**Example of a complete vision statement:**

> "By 2028, [Company] will serve 15,000 independent restaurant owners across North America with a fully integrated operations platform — so that every independent restaurant owner has the same operational visibility and financial control as a franchise operator, without giving up their independence."

This vision has a specific metric (15,000 owners), a time horizon (2028), a customer (independent restaurant owners, North America), a product outcome (fully integrated operations platform), and a "so that" clause that connects to purpose.

---

## What Does NOT Belong in a Vision Statement

**Values:** "We will be the most ethical company in our space" is a values statement, not a vision. Values belong in the purpose/culture layer, not the vision. A vision describes WHERE you are going, not HOW you will behave along the way.

**Strategy:** "We will achieve this by building a marketplace that connects X to Y" is strategy. Vision describes the destination; strategy describes the path. Keep them separate or you'll conflate what you're trying to achieve with how you're trying to achieve it.

**Vague aspirations:** "Be world-class," "be the leader," "set the standard" — these are aspirations without specificity. They cannot be tested, they don't guide decisions, and they don't create the tension between current state and future state that makes vision motivating.

**Activities:** "We will launch 5 new products" is an activity goal, not a vision. Vision describes outcomes in the world, not things the company will do.

## The Specificity Test

Before finalizing a vision statement, apply this diagnostic:

**Can you schedule the test?**
Look at the vision. Pick the deadline year. Put a reminder in your calendar for January of that year. When the reminder fires, will you be able to answer "did we achieve it, or didn't we?" If yes, it's a vision. If the answer would be "kind of, it depends on how you look at it," the vision needs more specificity.

**Can you draw the company?**
Imagine the company on the day the vision is achieved. Can you describe: how many people work there? What is the revenue? What does a customer say when asked what [Company] does? If you cannot paint this picture in concrete detail, the vision is not yet concrete enough.

**Does it require something you don't currently have?**
A vision that requires no new capabilities, no new markets, no new products, and no new operational maturity is not a vision — it's a forecast. The vision should demand capabilities the company is not certain it can build, growth rates that aren't guaranteed, or market positions that will require defeating well-resourced incumbents.

## Anti-Patterns in Vision Design

| Anti-Pattern | Example | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| **Too Short** | "Be #1 in our market" | No specificity, no time horizon, no customer context, doesn't guide decisions |
| **Too Long** | A paragraph describing the company's products, values, customers, and aspirations | Cannot be remembered, cannot be tested, conflates multiple strategic elements |
| **Too Vague** | "Become world-class in everything we do" | Not testable, excludes nothing, motivates no one |
| **Too Safe** | "Grow 15% annually for three years" | A financial projection, not a vision; doesn't stretch the organization |
| **Impossible** | "Be the largest company in the world in five years" | Demoralizes when it becomes clear it can't happen; damages credibility of planning process |
| **Purpose Disguised as Vision** | "Exist to help every human reach their potential" | This is a purpose statement; visions need specificity and time horizon |
| **Activity-Based** | "Launch in 5 new countries and release 12 major product updates" | Describes what the company will do, not what it will achieve or how the world will be different |

## Vision Crafting Process

### Step 1: Start from Purpose
Pull up the purpose statement from Phase 3A. The vision must be traceable to purpose. If you can imagine achieving the vision while completely abandoning the purpose, the vision is off-target.

### Step 2: Identify the 3-Year State
Using the Deiss framework, answer all four questions: revenue, customer/volume, market position, operational maturity. Write these as declarative statements, not goals. "We will be..." not "we hope to be..."

### Step 3: Work Backwards
From the 3-year state, ask: what has to be true in Year 2 for Year 3 to be achievable? What has to be true in Year 1 for Year 2 to be achievable? What has to happen in the next 90 days for Year 1 to be on track? This backwards-mapping reveals whether the vision is internally coherent or aspirational hand-waving.

### Step 4: Make It Vivid
Translate the four Deiss answers into a narrative. Read it aloud. Does it create a clear mental picture? Could a new employee read it and understand exactly where the company is going? Could a customer read it and understand what the company is becoming?

### Step 5: Test the Vision
Apply the three tests: the Specificity Test (can you schedule the test?), the Stretch Test (does it require capabilities you don't have?), and the Credibility Test (does your leadership team believe it's achievable?).

### Step 6: Cascade to Functions
A company vision is only actionable when functions ask "what does our team need to be true in 3 years for the company vision to be achievable?" This produces functional visions — the Customer Success team's 3-year picture, the Product team's 3-year picture, etc. Each must be traceable to the company vision.

## Vision Statements by Business Stage

| Stage | Revenue Range | Vision Example | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Seed** | <$1M | "By 2027, we will have 500 paying customers in the professional services market, each achieving measurable ROI within 90 days of onboarding, with $3.5M ARR and a team of 12 that can scale without the founders in every deal." | Specific customers, specific metric, specific operational goal — vivid for a small team deciding what to build |
| **Scale-up** | $1M–$5M | "By 2028, [Company] will be the default workforce management platform for healthcare staffing agencies with 50–500 contractors, with $15M ARR and NPS consistently above 60." | Names the customer precisely, names the market position, names the metric, names the quality bar |
| **Growth** | $5M–$30M | "By 2029, [Company] will operate in 8 countries, serve 200 enterprise customers averaging $250K ACV, generate $50M in ARR, and have a productized delivery model that requires fewer than 20 hours of professional services per implementation." | Multi-dimensional, operational specificity, shows the business model evolution |
| **Established** | $30M+ | "By 2030, [Company] will be the market-defining platform in [category] — recognized by analysts as the category leader, trusted by 40% of the Fortune 500, and generating $300M in ARR with 80% gross margin." | Analyst recognition (qualitative), customer penetration (quantitative), financial profile — tests whether they've built a category or just a company |

## Vision and OKRs: How They Connect

A common confusion: executives treat the vision as the place where OKRs live. This is a category error that degrades both vision and OKRs.

**Vision** is the destination — the specific state of the company at the end of the time horizon. It does not change quarter-to-quarter.

**OKRs** are the quarterly milestones on the road to the destination. They change every quarter as priorities shift and conditions evolve.

The connection:
- Each OKR cycle (quarterly) should contribute toward annual strategic goals
- Annual strategic goals should be the "what has to be true this year" milestone on the path to the vision
- Every 12–18 months, the vision should be reviewed: are we on track? Has the market changed in ways that require a vision update?

An OKR that cannot be traced to a strategic goal is noise. A strategic goal that cannot be traced to the vision is distraction. A vision that cannot be traced to purpose is arbitrary.
